'''Margaret Wetherby Williams''' (1901–1984),<ref name=WorldCat">{{cite web|title=Erskine, Margaret 1901–1984|publisher=WorldCat|url=https://www.worldcat.org/wcidentities/lccn-no2019084877|access-date=December 30, 2019}}</ref>, who wrote under the [[pseudonym]] '''Margaret Erskine''', was a British writer of [[crime fiction|mysteries]]. Her more than 20 novels featured the same leading character, Inspector Septimus Finch.<ref name="Crime and Mystery Writers">{{cite book|last=Becker|first=Mary Helen| chapter=Erskine, Margaret|editor=John M. Reilly|title=Twentieth-Century Crime and Mystery Writers|publisher=MacMillan|year=1980|pp=533–35|isbn=0-333-30107-2}}</ref> She was a member of [[PEN International]] and the [[Crime Writers' Association]].<ref name="Contemporary Authors">{{cite web|title=Margaret Wetherby Williams|work=Contemporary Authors Online|publisher=Gale|year=2011|url=https://link.gale.com/apps/doc/H1000106699/BIC?u=|url-access=subscription|access-date=December 28, 2019}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Born in [[Kingston, Ontario]], Canada, Williams grew up in [[Devon, England]].<ref name="Crime and Mystery Writers"/> Her parents were Thomas and Elizabeth (Erskine) Williams. She was privately educated.<ref name="Contemporary Authors"/> She died in 1984.<ref name=WorldCat"/>

==Critical reception==
Mary Helen Becker, writing in ''Twentieth Century Crime and Mystery Writers'', says that the Septimus Finch novels often feature large eccentric families, old houses, secret rooms, psychic phenomena, and "familiar puzzles". She rates the novels as "not really in the first rank" but "nevertheless better than many... Horrible things happen, but they are packaged for polite society."<ref name="Crime and Mystery Writers"/>
