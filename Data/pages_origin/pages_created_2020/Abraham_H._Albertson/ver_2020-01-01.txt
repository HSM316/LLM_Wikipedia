Abraham H. Albertson was one of [[Seattle, Washington]]'s most prominent architects of the first half of the 20th century. He was born in [[New Jersey]] and educated at [[Columbia University]] in New York. Early in his career, he moved to Seattle in the employ of a well-know New York architectural firm with that was developing a large area in downtown. He was associated with many projects in Seattle from around 1910 through the 20s and early 30s. Some of his designs are [[List of Seattle landmarks|Seattle landmarks]] and/or listed on the [[National Register of Historic Places]].

==Early life==
Albertson was born April 14, 1872 in [[Hope, New Jersey]]{{r|bio2}} to New Jersey natives Edward H. Albertson, a grocer, and Victoria Newman Albertson. In 1880, he was living in [[Hackettstown, New Jersey|Hackettstown]] and subsequently lived in New York City for ten years including time attending [[Columbia University]]. He served in [[Cuba]] during the [[Spanish-American War]] as a corporal in the U.S. Army and New York State National Guard with. He saw service with under [[Col. Theodore Roosevelt]] at the [[Battle of San Juan Hill]] and the [[Siege of Santiago]].{{r|bio1}} After the war, he returned to complete his education and graduated, with scholarship assistance, from the [[Columbia School of Architecture]] with a [[Bachelor of Philosophy|Ph.B]]{{r|bio2}} in 1895.{{r|sb1}} 

==Career==
He worked in New York City for the architectural firm [[Clinton and Russell]], being one of its earliest employees after it opened in 1894 when he started as a [[draftsman]]{{r|bio2}}. He moved to [[Duluth, Minnesota]] in 1905{{r|bio2}} and then to Seattle in 1907 as the local representative of the New York City [[Howells and Stokes]] until it dissolved in 1917. He was joined in the Seattle office by Joseph W. Wilson and Paul D. Richardson, who became his future partners.{{r|bio2}}

He remained in Seattle and was a partner or principal in several firms - Howells and Albertson (1920-28) (which succeeded the local office of Howells and Stokes after that firm closed); Albertson and Associates (1920-1933), and Albertson, Wilson, and Richardson (1935-1937), which had little work due to the [[Great Depression]]. His final position was with the federal government, as chief architect of the Washington State office of the [[Federal Housing Authority]] from 1939 until his retirement in 1949.{{r|bio1}}

==Projects==
He was the local representative of Howells and Stokes who was hired to plan the redevelopment of the original downtown Seattle site of the the [[University of Washington]]. The [[Metropolitan Tract]] was, at the time, the largest development of a downtown site undertaken in the United States.{{r|nc}} Albertson also supervised other work of Howells and Stokes in the west, including San Francisco's [[Royal Insurance Building, San Francisco|Royal Insurance Building]]. After the demise of Howells and Stokes, Albertson (with Wilson and Richardson) continued on to complete most of the remaining buildings in the Metropolitan Tract.{{r|bio3}}

He also developed a significant modification to the waterfront for a new Seattle Railroad and Marine Terminal, but the project was abandoned due to more pressing needs of [[World War I]].{{r|bio2}}

Albertson supervised the construction of the White-Henry-Stuart Building (1923), [[Cobb Building (Seattle)|Cobb Building]], Stimson Building (1925) and the Metropolitan Theatre.{{r|bio1}}

World War I brought Albertson government work including the Navy Yard Hotel in [[Bremerton]], and various apartments, houses, and schools.{{r|bio2}}

Some of his best known designs include the [[Northern Life Tower]] built in (1927-29), the downtown YMCA (1929-31), [[St. Joseph’s Church and Cornish School]] (1920-21), the Mrs. Grant Smith residence at 619 W. Comstock Street (a designated [[List of Seattle landmarks|Seattle landmark]], now the [[Stuart/Balcom House]]), St. Anne’s Convent (1930), and an addition to the former Children’s Orthopedic Hospital.{{r|shs}}

The architectural styles producted by Albertson varied greatly, including [[Spanish Revival]], [[Art Deco architecture|Art Deco]], and [[Collegiate Gothic]].{{r|bio3}}

==Other==
He became a member of the [[American Institute of Architects]] in 1910 and was once the national director. He was a chairman Seattle's building code committee, wrote the Seattle Tenement House Law, and was a director of the Seattle Social Welfare League, chairman of the Bi-state Federal Historical Monuments Survey, vice-chairman of the Washington State Emergency Public Works Board among other public positions.{{r|bio1}}

Seattle's [[University Street station]] contains a staircase with quotations inscribed on the risers from Albertson describing the Cobb Tower, served by the station and located just across University Street.{{r|ma}}

==Personal==
Albertson married Claire D. Fox, who was born in [[Illinois]] {{circa|1872}}, in [[Kitsap County, Washington]] in 1915. After her death in 1946, Albertson married Elizabeth G. Henry in Seattle. Abertson died April 18, 1964 in Seattle after a short illness.{{r|bio1}}
