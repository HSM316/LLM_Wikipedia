The following events occurred in '''May 1921''':

==May 1, 1921 (Sunday) ==
*[[1921 Jaffa riots|Riots began in Jaffa]], at the time in the British Mandate for Palestine. The activities of the [[Jewish Communist Party (Palestine)|Jewish Communist Party]], calling for the establishment of a "[[Soviet Union|Soviet]] Palestine", culminated in a march through the Jewish-Arab border neighborhood of [[Al-Manshiyya, Jaffa|Manshiyya]], where they clashed with the rival socialist [[Ahdut HaAvoda]] group, leading to large-scale rioting involving Jews, Christians, Arabs and Muslims.<ref name=segev>{{cite book |last=Segev |first=Tom |author-link=Tom Segev |title=One Palestine, Complete |year=1999 |publisher=Metropolitan Books |isbn=0-8050-4848-0 |pages=[https://archive.org/details/onepalestinecomp00sege/page/173 173–190] |url-access=registration |url=https://archive.org/details/onepalestinecomp00sege/page/173 }}</ref> Within days, at least 40 people had been killed. <ref>"Jaffa Fighting Continues", ''The New York Times'', May 5, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*The U.S. Navy opened a commercial wireless service between North America and Indo-China.<ref name=AROR521>[https://www.google.com/books/edition/The_American_Review_of_Reviews/DI1RAAAAYAAJ?hl=en&gbpv=1&bsq=%22current%20events%22%7C ''The American Review of Reviews''] Volume 63 (June, 1921) pp585-590</ref>

==May 2, 1921 (Monday) ==
[[File:III powstanie sląskie.jpg|150px|thumb|Bridge near Oppeln destroyed during the "Operation Bridges"]]
*The [[Silesian Uprisings#Third Silesian Uprising (1921)|third Silesian civil war]] began with "Operation Bridges", in which the [[Wawelberg Group]] (Polish forces, under the leadership of [[Wojciech Korfanty]]), destroyed German rail bridges near Neustadt, Kreuzburg, Cosel, Oppeln and Schwientochlowitz (now the Polish cities of [[Prudnik]], [[Kluczbork]], [[Kędzierzyn-Koźle]], [[Opole]] and [[Świętochłowice]]).<ref>{{cite web|url=http://wyborcza.pl/1,86176,2682777.html|title=2 V 1921. III powstanie śląskie|date=2 May 2005|author=Włodzimierz Kalicki
|language=pl|website=wyborcza.pl|access-date=31 December 2019}}</ref>  In response, the Interallied Commission declared a martial law for the urban and rural districts of Beuthen, Ratabor, Pless, Rybnick, Hindenburg and Tarnowitz (now the Polish cities of [[Bytom]], [[Raciborz]], [[Pszczyna]], [[Rybnik]], [[Zabrze]] and [[Tarnowskie Góry]]) <ref>"Puts Upper Silesia Under Martial Law", ''The New York Times'', May 4, 1921, p. 3</ref>
*[[Giovanni Ancillotto]] flew across the [[Andes]] in [[Peru]] in an [[Ansaldo A.1 Balilla]], making the journey from [[Lima]] to [[Cerro de Pasco]] in 1 hour 35 minutes, at an average altitude of 5,500 meters (18,044 feet), reached a maximum altitude of 7,000 meters (22,966 feet) while passing [[Ferrocarril Central Andino#History|Mount Meiggs]]; he covered the 76 miles from Lima to [[La Oroya]] at an average speed of 230&nbsp;km/hr (143&nbsp;mph).<ref>{{cite web|url=https://pueblomartir.wordpress.com//?s=Ancilotto&search=Ir|title=LA AVIACIÓN EN EL CERRO DE PASCO (Cuarta parte)|language=es|trans-title=THE AVIATION IN THE CERRO DE PASCO (Part Four)|date=July 31, 2015|work=PUEBLO MÁRTIR – César Pérez Arauco}}</ref>
*U.S. Secretary of State [[Charles Evans Hughes]] sent a memorandum to Germany announcing that the U.S. would not serve as a moderator in the reparations dispute.<ref name=AROR521/>
*General [[Jean Degoutte]] of France concentrated nine divisions of French Army troops on the border with Germany in preparation of occupying the Ruhr valley.<ref name=AROR521/>
*The U.S. Supreme Court reversed the conviction of U.S. Senator [[Truman Handy Newberry|Truman Newberry]] of Michigan for violation of the [[Federal Corrupt Practices Act]].<ref name=AROR521/>
*The Japanese passenger ship ''Tokuyo Maru'' caught fire and sank in the [[Pacific Ocean]]. The U.S. Army transport ship [[USAT Buford|USAT ''Buford'']] rescued 65 survivors.<ref>"Buford Rescues Crew of Japanese Freighter— United States Transport Saves Seamen From Burning Ship Out in the Pacific", ''The New York Times'', May 3, 1921, p. 5</ref><ref>{{Cite newspaper The Times |title=Imperial and Foreign News Items |date=5 May 1921 |page=9 |issue=42712 |column=G }}</ref>
*'''Born:''' [[Satyajit Ray]], Indian filmmaker, screenwriter, music composer, graphic artist, lyricist and author, in Calcutta<ref>{{Cite book|last1=Seton|given1=Marie|author-link=Marie Seton|year=1971|title=Satyajit Ray: Portrait of a director|publisher=Indiana University Press|isbn=978-0-253-16815-3|url-access=registration|url=https://archive.org/details/portraitofdirect0000seto|page=36}}</ref> (died 1992)
*'''Died:''' [[Yosef Haim Brenner]], 39, Russian-born Hebrew writer, murdered in the [[Jaffa Riots]].<ref>{{cite book|author=Anita Shapira|title=Yosef Haim Brenner: A Life|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=yE1IBQAAQBAJ&pg=PA366|date=17 December 2014|publisher=Stanford University Press|isbn=978-0-8047-9313-1|pages=363–366}}</ref>

==May 3, 1921 (Tuesday) ==
*The province of [[Northern Ireland]] was created within the United Kingdom from the six predominantly Protestant counties of [[Ulster]] [[Provinces of Ireland|Province]] (Antrim, Armagh, Down, Fermanagh, Londonderry and Tyrone), under the [[Government of Ireland Act 1920|Government of Ireland Act]].<ref>[[Alvin Jackson (historian)|Alvin Jackson]], ''Home Rule – An Irish History'', Oxford University Press, 2004, pp368-370.</ref>
[[File:John Wingate Weeks, Bain bw photo portrait.jpg|100px|thumb|right|Secretary Weeks]]
*U.S. Secretary of War [[John W. Weeks]] announced that all [[military conscription|draft]] [[draft evasion|evaders]] of the recent [[World War I|World War]] would be arrested, and that he would issue a list of willful deserters.<ref>"Will Bring to Trial Every Draft Evader; Weeks Says It Is Only in Justice to Millions Who Responded for Service", ''The New York Times'', May 4, 1921, p. 10</ref>  The published lists proved to be an embarrassment to Weeks after it was clear that they hadn't been verified <ref>"More Mistakes in the Slacker List", ''The New York Times'', May 7, 1921, p. 1</ref><ref>"Navy Officer Listed as Draft Dodger; Weeks Apologizes", ''The New York Times'', May 8, 1921, p. 1</ref><ref>"Move in Congress to Halt Draft Lists; Senator Stanley Introduces Bill to Compel Proof of Guilt Before Publication", ''The New York Times'', May 21, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*[[U.S. Steel Corporation]] announced that it was reducing the wages of 150,000 day laborers by 20%, with salary cuts to take place on May 16.  Wages, which had been raised in early 1918 because of the shortage of workers due to World War One, were returned to their pre-war level.  The minimum wage rate for a U.S. Steel employee was changed from 46 cents per hour to 36 cents per hour.<ref>"U.S. Steel Reduces Wages of 150,000 about 20 Per Cent", ''The New York Times'', May 4, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*The third population census of the population of the [[Union of South Africa]] was enumerated.<ref>{{cite book|author=Public Affairs Information Service|title=Public Affairs Information Service Bulletin|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=gQUdAQAAMAAJ|year=1926|publisher=Public Affairs Information Service|page=343}}</ref>  According to the final enumeration, the population of the minority-ruled Union in 1921 was 6,927,403 of whom 1,521,343 were white and 5,406,060 were non-whites.<ref>[https://www.google.com/books/edition/The_Statesman_s_Year_Book_1986_87/QunIDQAAQBAJ?hl=en&gbpv=1&dq=6927403&pg=PA1067&printsec=frontcover "South Africa: Area and Population", in ''The Statesman's Year-Book 1986-87'', ed. by John Paxton (The Macmillan Press Ltd, 1986) p1067]</ref>
*The government of [[France]] called up 200,000 men in preparation for the occupation of Germany's [[Ruhr Valley]].<ref>{{cite book|title=To the Masses: Proceedings of the Third Congress of the Communist International, 1921|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=PFLoBgAAQBAJ&pg=PA1111|date=13 February 2015|publisher=BRILL|isbn=978-90-04-28803-4|pages=1111}}</ref>
*The U.S Senate passed the Dillingham Immigration Bill, similar to one vetoed by President Wilson in February, by a vote of 78 to 1 in favor.<ref>"Immigration Bill Passed by Senate", ''The New York Times'', May 4, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*'''Born:''' [[Sugar Ray Robinson]], U.S. boxer, in Ailey, Georgia<ref>Robinson, Sugar Ray, and [[Dave Anderson (sportswriter)|Anderson, Dave]]. ''Sugar Ray'', London: Da Capo Press, 1994 {{ISBN|0-306-80574-X}} Page 7</ref> (died 1989)
*'''Died: '''[[William Robert Brooks]], 77, British-born American astronomer who discovered 27 comets during his career <ref>"Dr. W. R. Brooks Dies; Noted Astronomer", ''The New York Times'', May 4, 1921, p. 2</ref>

==May 5, 1921 (Thursday) ==
*The Allied [[Supreme War Council]] notified Germany of a default on the May 1 payment due for 12 billion gold marks and announced that Germany would have until May 12 to accept a total debt of 135 billion marks (equivalent to £6.75 billion or $33.75 billion), to be "paid in an indeterminate number of annual installments" worth of gold. British Prime Minister [[David Lloyd George]] handed the ultimatum, signed by himself and representatives of France, Belgium, Italy and Japan to Germany's Ambassador Friedrich Sthamer.<ref>"Ultimatum Presented to Germany; Must Yield Before May 12", ''The New York Times'', May 6, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*Sir [[James Craig, 1st Viscount Craigavon|James Craig]], leader of the [[Ulster Unionist Party]] of [[Northern Ireland]], and [[Éamon de Valera]], leader of the [[Sinn Féin]] party of Ireland, met in conference.<ref>"Ulster's Premier Meets De Valera", ''The New York Times'', May 6, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*The lowest attendance in the history of [[The Football League]] was recorded when only 13 paying spectators attended a football match between [[Leicester City F.C.|Leicester City]] and [[Stockport County F.C.]] in England. (However, the number of non-paying spectators at the match may have been between 1,000 and 2,000.)<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.footballsite.co.uk/Statistics/Articles/GroundsDoupleUp.htm|title=Two grounds have doubled up on staging League matches on the same day|work=footballsite.co.uk|access-date=2012-05-08|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130731080311/http://www.footballsite.co.uk/Statistics/Articles/GroundsDoupleUp.htm|archive-date=2013-07-31|url-status=dead}}</ref>

==May 9, 1921 (Monday) ==
*[[Japan]]'s Crown Prince and future Showa Emperor, [[Hirohito]], became the first member of Japanese royalty to visit the United Kingdom, arriving at [[Portsmouth]] where he was greeted by [[Edward VIII|Edward, Prince of Wales]], heir to the British throne and future King [[Edward VIII]].  The two heirs apparent then traveled by train to Victoria Station in London where they were greeted by [[George V of the United Kingdom|King George V]] and [[George VI|Albert, Duke of York]], the future King George VI.<ref>"Ovation in London for Prince Hirohito", ''The New York Times'', May 10, 1921, p. 14</ref>
*[[Albert Einstein]] was honored at [[Princeton University]] with the honorary degree of [[Doctor of Science]] during his visit to the United States.  Praising Einstein, university president [[John Grier Hibben]] said "In his structural theory of our ever-old, ever-new universe, his name stands latest in that illustrious series wherein the other moderns are Clark Maxwell, Sir Isaac Newton and Galileo, and the earliest name is Pythagoras," and added, "So, today, for his genius and integrity, we, who inadequately measure his power, salute the new Columbus of science, 'voyaging through strange seas of thought alone.' "<ref>"Einstein Receives Princeton Degree", ''The New York Times'', May 10, 1921, p. 14</ref>

Frankhauser, in Congress, a Suicide", ''The New York Times'', May 10, 1921, p. 6</ref> Frankhauser had been admitted to the [[Battle Creek Sanitarium]] in [[Battle Creek, Michigan]], because of his poor health.

==May 11, 1921 (Wednesday) ==
*Germany sent a note unconditionally accepting the reparation terms described in the ultimatum of May 5.  In London, German Ambassador Friedrich Sthamer delivered the note of acceptance to Prime Minister David Lloyd George, stating the German government had resolved "to carry out without reserve  or condition its obligations" to guarantee reparations, partially disarm its armed forces and to put accused war criminals on trial in German courts.<ref>"Germany's Surrender Unconditional; Commons Cheers News by Premier; France Ready to Keep Army Ready to Advance", ''The New York Times'', May 12, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*Thousands of people rioted in [[Kanchrapara]] after workers on the [[Eastern Bengal Railway|Eastern Bengal State Railway]] in [[British India]] went on strike.<ref>{{cite book|author=Sir Chettur Sankaran Nair|title=Gandhi and Anarchy|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=tHFnDwAAQBAJ&pg=PT205|date=1 January 2000|publisher=Chettur Sankaran Nair Foundation|pages=205}}</ref>
*British cotton weavers and spinners had their wages reduced by 30% by their employers.<ref name=AROR521/>
*Newspapers across North America, including ''The New York Times'', printed what turned out to be a false report from the agent for comedian [[Charlie Chaplin]] that he had been "severely burned" during the filming of his latest movie, ''[[The Idle Class]]''.  According to the account, "An acetylene torch used in the scene set Chaplin's coat and voluminous trousers afire.  In a second he was aflame from head to foot" and "was saved from fatal injury by employees, who wrapped him in wet blankets."<ref>"Charlie Chaplin Is Painfully Burned When He Upsets Torch in Making New Picture", ''The New York Times'', May 11, 1921, p. 1</ref><ref>"Chaplin, Film Comedian, Is Badly Burned", ''Chicago Tribune'', May 11, 1921, p. 1</ref><ref>"Charlie Chaplin Hurt— Painfully But Not Seriously Burned in Studio", ''Montreal Gazette'', May 11, 1921, p. 1</ref> Chaplin would write later that after a slight accident with a blowtorch requiring him to add "another layer of [[asbestos]]" to his outfit, his agent exaggerated the matter.  "Carl Robinson saw an opportunity for publicity... That evening I was shocked to read headlines that I had been severely burnt about the face, hands and body.... I issued a denial, but few newspapers printed it."  <ref>Charlie Chaplin, ''My Autobiography'' (The Bodley Head, 1964; reprinted by Melville House Publishing, 2012)</ref>  The papers that did print a correction generally did so as a less-prominently displayed followup.<ref>"Grieves for His Trousers", ''The New York Times'', May 12, 1921, p. 22</ref>

==May 12, 1921 (Thursday) ==
*[[Dealul Spirii Trial|The mass arrest of 51 Romanian communists]] was carried out in [[Bucharest]] at the conclusion of a convention of the [[Socialist Party of Romania]], on orders of Interior Minister [[Constantin Argetoianu]].  Four days earlier, [[Gheorghe Cristescu]] and other members of the ''Partidul Socialist'' who were in favor of agreeing to ally with the program of [[Communist International]] (Comintern) had voted to create a separate faction, the [[Romanian Communist Party|''Partidul Comunist Român'']].  With the approval of Prime Minister [[Alexander Averescu]], party leader Cristescu and other leaders were imprisoned for eight months before being given a trial, while 220 other Romanian communists were arrested and charged in a roundup.  After the conviction of 261 people, 213 would be granted amnesty by King Ferdinand on June 6, 1922, while the other 48 would serve sentences of up to ten years.<ref>[https://web.archive.org/web/20130909002040/http://jurnalul.ro/scinteia/istoria-comunismului/autoritatile-pierd-procesul-59114.html "History of Romanian Communism"], Part 3: "Autoritatile pierd procesul" ("The Authorities Lose the Trial") by Marius Tucă, ''[[Jurnalul Național]]'' (''National Journal'') (Bucharest), October 13, 2004</ref>

==May 13, 1921 (Friday) ==
*Under the new [[Government of Ireland Act 1920|Home Rule Act]] for the [[Southern Ireland (1921–22)|26 predominantly Roman Catholic counties of Ireland]], [[1921 Irish elections|elections]] were held for the [[Parliament of Southern Ireland]].  The [[Sinn Fein]]'s 124 unopposed candidates captured all of the parliament, except for four [[Independent Unionist]] Party members from [[Dublin]] who had run unopposed.  Half of the winning candidates were incarcerated.  The remaining Sinn Fein winners, who had campaigned on a platform of Irish independence, stated that they would refuse the oath of allegiance to the Crown, part of the swearing-in ceremony.<ref>"Sinn Fein Sweeps Southern Ireland— Elects 124 Members to New Parliament, but They Won't Take Their Seats; Half of Them Are in Jail", ''The New York Times'', May 14, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*[[Rufus Isaacs, 1st Marquess of Reading|Lord Reading]], the [[Governor-General of India]], conferred with the [[Mahatma Gandhi]], leader of the [[Indian independence movement]], at a meeting in [[Simla]].<ref name=AROR621>[https://www.google.com/books/edition/The_American_Review_of_Reviews/7XdHAAAAYAAJ?hl=en&gbpv=1&bsq=current%20events ''The American Review of Reviews'', Volume 64 (July, 1921) pp25-28]</ref> 
*Komatsu Ironworks, a predecessor of [[Komatsu Limited|Komatsu]], a worldwide [[construction machinery]] and [[forklift]] brand, was founded in [[Ishikawa Prefecture]], Japan.<ref>[https://home.komatsu/en/company/profile/ "Corporate Profile"], Komatsu.com</ref>
*All seven of the crew on the U.S. schooner ''Miztec'' died when the ship foundered in [[Lake Superior]] during a blizzard, off [[Vermilion Point]], [[Michigan]].<ref>"Six Feared Dead in Wreck of Barge in Lake Storm", ''New York Tribune'', May 15, 1921, p. 1</ref>

==May 14, 1921 (Saturday) ==
*[[May 1921 geomagnetic storm|A geomagnetic storm, caused by a solar outburst]], began interfering with telegraph and telephone transmissions (and railroad signals) and causing an [[aurora borealis]] to be observed in the northeastern United States.  The activity was attributed by astronomers at the U.S. Naval Observatory to a [[sunspot]] {{convert|94000|mi}} long and {{convert|21000|mi}} wide.,<ref>"Sunspot Aurora Paralyzes Wires— Unprecedented Disturbance Is Attributed to Solar Manifestations", ''The New York Times'', May 15, 1921, p. 1</ref> lasting until May 17 and causing damage in North America, Europe and the southern hemisphere.<ref>{{cite book|title=Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Ci1DAQAAMAAJ|year=1922|publisher=Priestley and Weale|page=556}}</ref><ref>"Magnetic Tremors Expected to Pass Within 48 Hours", ''The New York Times'', May 16, 1921, p. 1</ref><ref>"Cables Damaged by Sunspot Aurora", ''The New York Times'', May 17, 1921, p. 1</ref>

*U.S. stunt pilot [[Laura Bromwell]] set a new record for consecutive [[Aerobatic maneuver|loops]] by a woman, with a total of 199 consecutive loops in 1 hour 20 minutes over [[Roosevelt Field (airport)|Roosevelt Field]] near [[Mineola, New York]] on Long Island.  She was killed in a plane crash three weeks later.<ref>Gunston, Bill, ed., ''Aviation: Year by Year'', London: Amber Books Limited, 2001, cited at [http://www.wingsoverkansas.com/history/article.asp?id=1039 Wings Over Kansas: Aviation History: Aviation History Facts: May] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120204192121/http://www.wingsoverkansas.com/history/article.asp?id=1039 |date=2012-02-04 }}</ref><ref>"Girl Flier Makes 199 Loop the Loops", ''The New York Times'', May 16, 1921, p. 7</ref>
*'''Born:''' [[Baron Vaea]] (Siaosi Tuʻihala ʻAlipate Vaea Tupou), [[Prime Minister of Tonga]] from 1991 to 2000<ref>{{cite book|author=Roger East|title=Whitaker's Almanack World Heads of Government, 1998|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=S5COPB35fCoC|year=1998|publisher=Stationery Office|isbn=978-0-11-702205-8|page=259}}</ref> (died 2009)

==May 16, 1921 (Monday) ==
*The [[Communist Party of Czechoslovakia]] was founded at a conference in [[Prague]].<ref>{{cite book|title=Czechoslovak Foreign Trade|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=-TJlI5O6kB4C|year=1981|publisher=Rapid, Czechoslovak Advertising Agency|page=6}}</ref>  Meeting in [[Prague]], former Social Democrats met as delegates to a congress of the Czechoslovak Social Democrats of the Left (''Československé sociálně demokratické strany dělnické, levice'' or ''Česko-slovenskej sociálnodemokratickej strany, ľavicovej'') voted, 562 to 7, to join [[Comintern]].<ref>"Czechs Turn to Lenin", ''The New York Times'', May 18, 1921, p. 3</ref>
*The U.S. Supreme Court decided that the capital gains tax (a tax on the increased value of corporate assets) could be assessed and included in profits for purposes of calculating taxable revenue.<ref name=AROR621/>  The Court also rejected a challenge to the ratification clause of the 18th Amendment for prohibition of the manufacture, transport and sale of alcohol.<ref name=AROR621/>

==May 17, 1921 (Tuesday) ==
*The UK's [[Ministry of Labour (United Kingdom)|Ministry of Labour]], under [[Thomas Macnamara]], issued an order confirming general minimum time-rates, piece-work basis time-rates and overtime rates for male and female workers.<ref>{{cite book|title=The Ministry of Labour Gazette|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=rvfmAAAAMAAJ|year=1921|publisher=H.M. Stationery Office|page=327}}</ref>
*The United States Bureau of the Census announced the final figures for the 1920 decennial census, adding 27,512 to the provisional number announced on October 7.  The final figure was 105,710,620 for the 48 states and 117,859,358 when including outlying U.S. territories.<ref>"Final Figures Put Population Of the Nation at 105,710,620", ''The New York Times'', May 18, 1921, p. 1</ref>

==May 18, 1921 (Wednesday) ==
[[File:Sidney Johnston Catts in 1916 (cropped).jpg|100px|thumb|right|Governor Catts]]
*Former Florida Governor [[Sidney J. Catts]] was indicted in the U.S. District Court at Pensacola for his policy of [[peonage]] (forced servitude to pay a debt) for African-Americans under his employ.  According to the indictment, Catts used his power of pardon to release two black men from the Florida state prison camp and had them transferred to his plantation in [[Walton County, Florida|Walton County]] for involuntary servitude.<ref>"Ex-Gov. Catts Indicted for Florida Peonage; Charge Pardoned Men Were Forced to Work", ''The New York Times'', May 19, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*'''Born:''' [[Patrick Dennis]], U.S. writer, in [[Chicago]]<ref>[[Cook County, Illinois]] Certificate of Birth No. 66648 https://familysearch.org/pal:/MM9.3.1/TH-267-12569-7046-12?cc=1462519</ref> (died 1976)
*'''Died: '''[[Franklin K. Lane]], 56, former U.S. Secretary of the Interior<ref>{{cite book|author=Cosmos Club (Washington, D.C.)|title=Annual Reports|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=nwxKAAAAMAAJ|year=1921|page=3}}</ref>

==May 19, 1921 (Thursday) ==
*U.S. President [[Warren G. Harding]] signed the [[Emergency Quota Act]], also known as the Dillingham Immigration Bill into law, limiting annual immigration from individual nations to no more than the number of people who had immigrated to America from those nations in 1910.<ref>{{cite web|title=1921 Emergency Quota Law (An act to limit the immigration of aliens into the United States)|url=http://library.uwb.edu/guides/usimmigration/1921_emergency_quota_law.html|website=US immigration legislation online|access-date=30 January 2015}}</ref>
*The first electrically propelled American ship, the ''Eclipse'', arrived back in New York after a successful {{convert|26500|mi}} voyage on electric power.<ref name=AROR621/>
*The Republic of China filed a protest against the United Kingdom after the UK elected to renew the Anglo-Japanese alliance, which had been set to expire on July 1.<ref name=AROR621/>
*[[Marie Curie]] was presented the Gold Medal of the [[National Institute of Social Sciences]] at a ceremony in New York.<ref>"Scientists' Medal Given Mme. Curie", ''The New York Times'', May 20, 1921, p. 3</ref>
[[File:EdwardDouglassWhite.jpeg|100px|thumb|right|Chief Justice White]]
*'''Died: '''[[Edward Douglass White]], 75, [[Chief Justice of the United States]] since 1910 and a member of the United States Supreme Court since 1894.<ref>"Chief Justice White Is Dead at Age of 75 After an Operation", ''The New York Times'', May 19, 1921, p. 1</ref>

==May 20, 1921 (Friday) ==
[[File:Alfredo Zayas y Alfonso 1925.jpg|100px|thumb|right|President Zayas]]
*Dr. [[Alfredo Zayas]] was inaugurated as the [[President of Cuba]].<ref>"Zayas Inaugurated as Cuban President", ''The New York Times'', May 21, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*[[Mingo County, West Virginia]] was placed under martial law by [[West Virginia]]'s Governor [[Ephraim F. Morgan]].<ref>"Mingo Feud Zone Under Martial Law", ''The New York Times'', May 21, 1921, p. 1</ref>

==May 21, 1921 (Saturday)==
*[[Wonder Bread]], one of the most popular brands of [[white bread]] sold in North America, was introduced by the Taggart Baking Company of [[Indianapolis]] after a promotion in newspaper ads and flyers that simply had the word "WONDER" <ref>[https://www.wonderbread.com/about-us "About Us"], Wonder Bread corporate website</ref><ref>advertisement for "Taggart's Wonder Bread", in ''Indianapolis News'', May 21, 1921, p.3</ref> and poems that gave no clue about what the "wonder" was.<ref>e.g., "Just remember this—you'll never find A WONDER of a better kind", ad in ''Indianapolis News'', May 20, 1921, p.48</ref>
*'''Born:''' 
**[[Andrei Sakharov]], Soviet physicist, dissident and peace campaigner; in [[Moscow]]<ref>{{cite news|title=Sakharov Human Rights Prize 25th anniversary marked in US|url=http://www.voanews.com/content/anniversary-of-andrei-sakharov-human-rights-prize/1830297.html|publisher=[[Voice of America]]|date=15 January 2014}}</ref> (d. 1989)
**[[Prabhat Ranjan Sarkar]], Indian spiritual [[guru]] and [[Bengali language]] musical composer; in [[Jamalpur, Bihar|Jamalpur]], [[Bihar and Orissa Province]], [[British India]] (d. 1990)

==May 22, 1921 (Sunday) ==
*''[[Shuffle Along]]'', the first [[musical theatre|musical revue]] to be written and performed by African Americans, was premiered on [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]].  With music and lyrics by [[Noble Sissle]] and [[Eubie Blake]], the show introduced the hit song "[[I'm Just Wild About Harry]]" and opened at the David Belasco Theater.<ref>"Harlem Renaissance", by Christopher George Buck, in ''Encyclopedia of Race, Ethnicity and Society", ed. by Richard T. Schaefer (SAGE Publications, 2008) p558</ref>

==May 23, 1921 (Monday) ==
*The [[Leipzig War Crimes Trials]] opened in Germany, starting with the trial of Sergeant Karl Heynen, the commandant of a prisoner of war camp in Münstereins in Westphalia, for his brutal treatment of British POWs, 16 of whom appeared as witnesses for the prosecution.  According to a report from the scene, "it was the first time the former soldiers had seen their tormentor since 1915."<ref>"Atrocities in Prison Camps Told in Trial", International News Service report in ''Buffalo Enquirer'', May 24, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*Rioting broke out at [[Alexandria]] in [[Egypt]], with 48 people killed and 191 injured <ref>"Alexandria Quieting Down— Rioting Casualties Are 12 Europeans and 36 Natives Killed; 191 Wounded", ''The New York Times'', May 25, 1921, p. 9</ref> before police suppressed the violence. The fighting had broken out on Sunday night when, according to an Associated Press report, "trouble started between low-class Greeks and natives on Anastasia Street from an unknown cause.  The indiscriminate fighting and revolver shooting there spread to other districts.<ref>"Egyptian Rioting Costs 37 Lives", ''The New York Times'', May 24, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*'''Born:''' [[Humphrey Lyttelton]], English jazz musician and broadcaster, in [[London]] (died 2008)<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/media/2008/apr/28/radio.bbc|title=Humphrey Lyttelton—Masterly jazz musician and broadcaster who chaired Radio 4's I'm Sorry I Haven't a Clue with wit and charm|author1=[[George Melly]]|author2=[[Jeremy Hardy|Hardy, Jeremy]] |author3=Fordham, John |date=28 April 2008|work=[[The Guardian]]|access-date=28 April 2008}}</ref>

==May 24, 1921 (Tuesday) ==
*In [[1921 Northern Ireland general election|Northern Ireland's first general election]], the [[Ulster Unionist Party]] won a [[two-thirds majority]] of votes cast and almost three-quarters of the 52 seats in the new [[Parliament of Northern Ireland]].<ref>"Unionists Sweep Ulster Counties; Belfast in Frenzy", ''The New York Times'', May 27, 1921, p. 1</ref><ref>{{cite book |last=Lynch |first=Robert |title=Revolutionary Ireland, 1912–25 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=RTfbBQAAQBAJ&q=despite+all+this+the+nationalist+hopes+for+the+election+were+high&pg=PA97 |publisher=Bloomsbury Publishing |year=2015 |pages=96–97 |isbn=9781441186898}}</ref>  The voting was held under the new Home Rule Act, which provided for separate parliaments for the Protestant counties in the north and the Roman Catholic counties in the south.<ref>"Belfast Fights, Parades and Votes in First Election", ''The New York Times'', May 25, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*U.S. Navy Rear Admiral [[Samuel Robison]] was appointed as the [[United States occupation of the Dominican Republic (1916–1924)|U.S. Military Governor of Santo Domingo]], now the [[Dominican Republic]], which was under American occupation and administration.<ref>"Robison to Govern in Santo Domingo", ''The New York Times'', May 25, 1921, p. 3</ref>

==May 25, 1921 (Wednesday) ==
[[File:19210525 - Dublin - Agence Rol 02.jpg|150px|thumb|right|Fire at The Custom House]]
*The [[Irish Republican Army]] occupied and burned [[The Custom House]] in [[Dublin]].<ref>"Sinn Feiners Burn Dublin Custom House; Fight Pitched Battle in the Streets; 18 Killed or Wounded, 111 Prisoners", ''The New York Times'', May 26, 1921, p. 1</ref> The violence resulted in the deaths of five Republicans, and the capture of another 80 by the [[British Army]].<ref>{{cite book|title=Michael Collins's Intelligence War|first=Michael T.|last=Foy|year=2006|isbn=0-7509-4267-3|pages=214–218}}</ref>
*'''Born:''' [[Hal David]], U.S. songwriter, in New York City (died 2012)<ref>{{cite news|last=Hoerburger|first=Rob|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2012/09/02/arts/music/hal-david-oscar-and-grammy-winning-songwriter-is-dead-at-91.html|title=Hal David, Songwriter, Is Dead at 91|access-date=1 September 2012|newspaper=The New York Times|date=1 September 2012}}</ref>
*'''Died:''' [[Émile Combes]], 85, [[Prime Minister of France]] 1902 to 1905<ref>{{EB1911|wstitle=Combes, Émile|volume=6|pages=751–752|inline=1}}</ref>

==May 26, 1921 (Thursday) ==
*France's Prime Minister [[Aristide Briand]] won a vote of confidence, 403 to 163, as a test of his policy of moderation toward Germany.<ref>"Briand Wins Fight in French Chamber by 403 Votes to 163", ''The New York Times'', May 27, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*In one of Germany's first war crimes convictions, under trials held at [[Leipzig]] since Germany had signed the [[Treaty of Versailles]], a German court convicted a former Army sergeant and imposed a ten month sentence.<ref name=AROR621/>
*A general strike was proclaimed in [[Norway]], in support of seamen threatened with a 30% wage cut.<ref>{{cite book|title=The Labour International Year Book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WDsuAQAAIAAJ|year=1923|page=100}}</ref>
[[File:Rickenbacker.jpg|150px|thumb|right|Rickenbacker]]
*American aviator [[Eddie Rickenbacker]] narrowly escaped getting killed when his airplane crashed in [[Cheyenne, Wyoming]], while he was attempting a transcontinental flight.  Rickenbacker had departed from [[Redwood City, California]] at 8:32 in the morning and, while over [[Reno, Nevada]] and [[Salt Lake City]], [[Utah]], he dropped leaflets over both cities, each a copy of a Memorial Day address made by the leader of the [[American Legion]].  Though prepared for a 24-hour flight to Washington DC, with {{convert|330|usgal}} of gasoline and 30 gallons of oil, Rickenbacker experienced trouble as he approached the Cheyenne airport and his airplane flipped over on a hard landing.<ref>"Rickenbacker Wrecks Plane in Long Flight— Aviator Narrowly Escapes Death in Landing at Cheyenne on Cross-Continent Trip", The New York Times'', May 27, 1921, p. 2</ref>  Using another plane, Rickenbacker completed his flight two days later <ref>"Rickenbacker Ends Continental Flight", ''The New York Times'', May 28, 1921, p. 6</ref> and would live 52 more years, passing away in 1973.

==May 27, 1921 (Friday) ==
*The discovery of the body of Anna Brown in a ravine in [[Osage County, Oklahoma]], United States, led to a large-scale murder investigation of the [[Osage Indian murders]], potentially involving hundreds of victims over a twenty-year period.<ref>Grann, David. Killers of the Flower Moon: the Osage Murders and the Birth of the FBI. p. 307-308. Vintage, 2017.</ref>
*The state of emergency in the [[United Kingdom]] was renewed by royal proclamation in response to the continuation of the miners' strike.<ref>{{cite book|author=Great Britain. Parliament. House of Lords|title=The Parliamentary Debates (official Report).: House of Lords|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=4Yh_Pk_N-ZwC|year=1921|publisher=H.M. Stationery Office|page=415}}</ref><ref>"Britain May Extend Irish Martial Law", ''The New York Times'', May 29, 1921, p. 3</ref>
*[[Mensheviks|Menshevik]] soldiers who called themselves the "Kappell troops" in honor of the late Menshevik General [[Vladimir Kappel]], seized control of the Bolshevik government in the eastern Russian port of [[Vladivostok]], flying the Russian Imperial flag at public buildings.  The Mensheviks had captured the city of [[Ussuriysk]] (at the time called Nikolsk) on May 21.<ref>"Anti-Bolsheviki Take Vladivostok; Fly Imperial Flag— Kappel Troops Are in Possession, Although Fighting Is Still Going On", ''The New York Times'', May 28, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*The [[Emergency_Tariff_of_1921|Emergency Tariff bill]] took effect immediately in the U.S. after being signed into law by President Harding.<ref name=AROR621/> 
*British Army troops arrived at [[Opole|Oppeln]], the capital of [[Upper Silesia]], in a region which had recently voted in a plebiscite to become part of Germany rather than Poland.  The peacekeeping force, meant to prevent fighting between the German and Polish ethnic communities, brought with it airplanes, tanks and other armored equipment.<ref name=AROR621/>

==May 28, 1921 (Saturday) ==
[[File:Curtiss Eagle I.jpg|100px|thumb|A Curtiss Eagle airliner]]
*All seven people aboard a U.S. Army [[Curtiss Eagle]] ambulance plane [[Curtiss Eagle#Operational history|were killed in the deadliest airplane accident in American history up to that time]].  The tri-motor biplane was on its way from [[Langley Air Force Base|Langley Field]] near [[Hampton, Virginia]] and was on a {{convert|140|mi}} trip to [[Joint Base Anacostia–Bolling#Bolling Field]] near Washington, D.C., flying when it crashed near [[Morgantown, Maryland]] during a violent thunderstorm, killing five U.S. Army officers and two civilians.  Earlier in the day, two U.S. Representatives ([[Philip P. Campbell]] of Kansas and [[Joseph Walsh (Massachusetts politician)|Joseph Walsh]] of Massachusetts) had accompanied Lieutenant Colonel Archie Miller and the pilot and mechanic of the plane to Langley, along with French Embassy official Guy de Lavergne, American Automobile Association chairman A. G. Batchelder and Curtiss Airplane Company sales manager Maurice Connolly.  The two Congressmen and the French diplomat had been airsick and elected not to make the return flight, and two second lieutenants boarded the plane.<ref>"Seven Die in Crash of Ambulance Plane As Potomac Storm Downs Army Fliers", ''The New York Times'', May 30, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*The Farmer's Finance Corporation was incorporated in Delaware from the merger of several farmers' organizations and started with capital of $100,000,000.<ref name=AROR621/>
*The [[Communist Party of Canada]] met for the first time, secretly, in a barn in [[Guelph, Ontario]].<ref>{{cite book|author=Gerry Van Houten|title=Canada's Party of Socialism: History of the Communist Party of Canada, 1921-1976|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=38gdAAAAMAAJ|year=1982|publisher=Progress Books|isbn=978-0-919396-46-3|page=xiv}}</ref>

==May 29, 1921 (Sunday) ==
*Voters in the [[Salzburg (state)|Salzburg province]] in [[Austria]] overwhelmingly voted in a [[plebiscite]] to be incorporated into Germany.  The results of the election, which had been conducted despite warnings from Chancellor [[Michael Mayr]] and from the Allied Supreme Council, were not recognized by Austria as binding.<ref>"Salzburg a Unit for German Union; Referendum Vote Taken Despite Chancellor's Warning", ''The New York Times'', May 30, 1921, p. 3</ref>
*The [[1921 Liège–Bastogne–Liège|11th Liège–Bastogne–Liège]] cycle race took place in Belgium and was won by [[Louis Mottiat]].<ref>{{cite book|author=Gérard Goy|title=Tours 1914 à 1925|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=lWjhCgAAQBAJ&pg=PA322|date=29 October 2015|publisher=Editions Publibook|isbn=978-2-342-04401-0|pages=321–2}}</ref>

==May 30, 1921 (Monday) ==
*Germany completed its latest annual payment of one billion gold marks to the Allied Reparations Commission, with a final deposit of twenty treasury notes worth ten million marks apiece, one day ahead of the scheduled May 31 deadline.<ref>"Germany Begins to Pay; Reparations Commission Receives the Billion Gold Marks Due Today", by Edwin L. James, ''The New York Times'', May 31, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*The All-Russian Communist Party Congress approved a proposal by Party Secretary [[Vladimir Lenin]] for economic reform that included limited capitalism for small businesses.  Finance for the Soviet government was made by a one-third tax on income, with peasants being assessed on the one-third value of their assets.  The Party maintained state control of the transportation, textile, leather and salt industries.<ref name=AROR621/> 
*Ethnic fighting between Upper Silesian Germans and Poles took place at [[Bytom|Beuthen]], whose residents had voted in favor of remaining part of Germany in the recent plebiscite.  There were 400 casualties.  The city would become part of Poland after World War II and renamed [[Bytom]].<ref>"Germans and Poles Break Armistice", ''The New York Times'', May 31, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*Seventeen underground miners at [[Meuselwitz]] in Germany were killed when a sudden downpour caused a flash flood of the [[Schnauder]] River.<ref>"Seventeen Persons Lose Lives As Storm Floods German Mine", ''The New York Times'', May 31, 1921, p. 1</ref>
*The [[1921 Indianapolis 500|Indianapolis 500]] was won by [[Tommy Milton]].<ref>{{cite book|last=Fox|first=Jack C.|title =The Illustrated History of the Indianapolis 500 1911-1994|publisher =Carl Hungness Publishing|edition=4th|date=1994|pages=22|language=en|isbn=0-915088-05-3}}</ref><ref>"Milton Captures 500-Mile Classic", ''The New York Times'', May 31, 1921, p. 21</ref>
*'''Born:''' [[Jamie Uys]], South African film director, in Boksburg<ref>{{cite book|author=Harris M. Lentz (III.)|title=Obituaries in the Performing Arts|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=pqwYAAAAIAAJ|year=1996|publisher=McFarland & Company|isbn=978-0-7864-0302-8|page=198}}</ref> (died 1996)

==May 31, 1921 (Tuesday) ==
*The [[Tulsa race riot]] began as white mobs attacked black residents and businesses in the [[Greenwood, Tulsa|Greenwood]] District of [[Tulsa]], [[Oklahoma]], United States. 26 black and 10 white people were killed; it is estimated that 150–200 black and 50 white people were injured.<ref>{{citation |author=Oklahoma Commission |contribution=Final Report |title=Oklahoma Commission to Study the Tulsa Race Riot of 1921 |place=Tulsa, Oklahoma |date=February 28, 2001 |contribution-url=http://www.okhistory.org/research/forms/freport.pdf |contribution-format=PDF |access-date=June 20, 2018}}</ref>
*The U.S. Railway Labor Board announced that railwaymen's wages would be reduced on July 1 by an average of 12%.<ref name=AROR621/>
