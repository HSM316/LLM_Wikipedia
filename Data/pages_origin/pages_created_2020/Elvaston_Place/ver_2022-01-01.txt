'''Elvaston Place''' is a street in [[South Kensington]], London.

Elvaston Place runs west to east from [[Gloucester Road, London|Gloucester Road]] to [[Queen's Gate]].

The [[Embassy of Gabon, London]] is at number 27. The [[High Commission of Mauritius, London]] is at number 32/33. The [[Embassy of Iraq, London]] has its consular section at number 3.

==History==
Much of the street, 1-20 and 32–46, was built by the property developer [[Charles Aldin]] in the early 1860s.<ref name="Survey of London">{{cite web |title=Domestic Buildings after 1851: The Italianate Tradition |url=https://www.british-history.ac.uk/survey-london/vol38/pp262-307 |website=Survey of London |accessdate=1 January 2020}}</ref> 26-31 were built in 1866–68.<ref name="Survey of London"/>

[[Miss Ironside's School]] was located at number 2.

==Notable residents==
In 1868, [[John Crawfurd]], Scottish physician, colonial administrator, diplomat, and author, died at his home in the street.

From 1872 to his death in 1897, Liberal MP and Cabinet Minister Rt Hon [[Anthony John Mundella|A J Mundella]] lived at number 16.

In 1882, [[William Bence Jones]], Anglo-Irish agriculturist, died at his home in the street.

In 1884, [[David Russell (British Army officer)|General Sir David Russell]] died at his home in the street.

Until his death in 1894, [[Patrick Leonard MacDougall|General Sir Patrick MacDougall]] lived at number 22.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/MacDougall,_Patrick_Leonard_(DNB01)|title=MacDougall, Patrick Leonard|publisher=Dictionary of National Biography, 1901 supplement}}</ref>

In 1895, [[George Brydges Rodney (Royal Marines officer)|Lieutenant General George Brydges Rodney]] died at his home in Elvaston Place.<ref>{{cite journal | title=Obituary | journal=The Annual Register | page=194 | year=1896 | url=https://books.google.com/books?id=g2ZdAAAAIAAJ&pg=RA1-PA194}}</ref>

In 1905, the artist [[Edward Burra]] was born there at his grandmother's house.<ref name=SMartin>{{Cite book|title=Edward Burra|last=Martin|first=Simon|publisher=Lund Humphries|year=2011|isbn=9781869827106|location=Surrey, UK|pages=14–15}}</ref>

In 1920, [[William Plunket, 5th Baron Plunket]] died at his home at number 40.<ref name="Te Ara">{{cite book|title=An Encyclopaedia of New Zealand|year=1966|publisher=Ministry for Culture and Heritage/Te Manatū Taonga|url=http://www.teara.govt.nz/en/1966/plunket-sir-william-lee-plunket-fifth-baron-gcvo-gcmg-kbe-kgstj-ba/1|editor=A. H. McLintock|accessdate=1 January 2020|chapter=PLUNKET, Sir William Lee Plunket, Fifth Baron, GCVO, GCMG, KBE, KGStJ, BA}}</ref>

In the late 1930s, [[Geoffrey Wilkinson]], the Nobel laureate English chemist, rented a room at number 4.<ref name="Gay2007">{{cite book|author=Hannah Gay|title=The History of Imperial College London, 1907-2007: Higher Education and Research in Science, Technology, and Medicine|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=2BpqDQAAQBAJ&pg=PA413|year=2007|publisher=World Scientific|isbn=978-1-86094-708-7|page=413}}</ref>

In 1941, the actor [[S. J. Warmington]] who lived at number 39, died when his neighbourhood was showered with [[Incendiary device|incendiary bombs]], after he went out to help extinguish fires and was killed when a high-explosive bomb fell.<ref name=cwgc>[http://www.cwgc.org/find-war-dead/casualty/3132483/WARMINGTON,%20STANLEY%20JAMES] CWGC Casualty Record, Kensington Metropolitan Borough.</ref>

In 1945, the actor [[Michael Hordern]] and his wife Eve Mortimer lived in a rented flat there.

From 1 January 1968 until at least May 1969, the musician [[Al Stewart]] lived in a basement flat at number 10, and his song ''Elvaston Place'' is about his time there.<ref name="Songfacts">{{cite web |title=Elvaston Place by Al Stewart |url=https://www.songfacts.com/facts/al-stewart/elvaston-place |website=Songfacts |accessdate=1 January 2020}}</ref>
