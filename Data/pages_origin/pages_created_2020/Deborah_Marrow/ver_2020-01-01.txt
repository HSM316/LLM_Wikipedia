'''Deborah Marrow''' (October 18, 1948 – October 1, 2019) was an American arts administrator, longtime director of the [[Getty Foundation]] at the [[J. Paul Getty Trust]].

== Early life ==
Marrow was born in New York City and raised in [[Scarsdale, New York]], the daughter of Seymour A. Marrow and Adele Marrow. Her father was an executive in the apparel industry.<ref name=":3">{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1971/06/20/archives/miss-marrow-wed-to-m-mcgure.html|title=Miss Marrow Wed To M. J. McGuire|date=1971-06-20|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-12-30|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> She graduated from the [[University of Pennsylvania]] in 1970, with a bachelor's degree in history. She earned a master's degree in art history at [[Johns Hopkins University]], and completed doctoral studies in art history at the University of Pennsylvania in 1978, with a dissertation about royal arts patron [[Marie de' Medici|Marie de’ Medici]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2019/10/09/arts/deborah-marrow-dead.html|title=Deborah Marrow, a Getty Leader With Global Reach, Dies at 70|last=Finkel|first=Jori|date=2019-10-09|work=The New York Times|access-date=2019-12-30|language=en-US|issn=0362-4331}}</ref><ref name=":4">{{Cite web|url=https://almanac.upenn.edu/articles/deborah-marrow-trustee-emerita|title=Deborah Marrow, Trustee Emerita|last=|first=|date=October 8, 2019|website=Almanac|language=en|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref>

== Career ==
During graduate school, Marrow was a research assistant at the [[Philadelphia Museum of Art]].<ref name=":4" /> Marrow moved to Los Angeles in 1977. She became editor of ''Chrysalis'', a feminist arts periodical that ceased production in 1980. In 1983 she began working on publications at the Getty Trust. <ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=https://www.artforum.com/news/deborah-marrow-1948-2019-80957|title=Deborah Marrow (1948–2019)|last=|first=|date=October 3, 2019|website=ArtForum|language=en-US|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref>

Marrow became director of the Getty Foundation, the grant-making program the J. Paul Getty Trust, in 1984. She was responsible for over $410 million in grants, to over a hundred arts institutions and scholars. "It gave me the opportunity to think about art and architecture across the entire world," she commented on her work with the Getty.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.theartnewspaper.com/news/deborah-marrow-the-getty-s-longest-serving-leader-has-died-aged-70|title=Deborah Marrow, the Getty’s longest-serving leader, has died, aged 70|last=Stoilas|first=Helen|date=October 2, 2019|website=The Art Newspaper|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref> 

Marrow created the Getty Multicultural Undergraduate Internship in 1993, as a response to riots and racial inequalities in Los Angeles. The internship funds undergraduates who would otherwise not be able to sustain a typically unpaid internship in a museum or other arts non-profit. Marrow was especially known for funding [[Pacific Standard Time: LA/LA|Pacific Standard Time]], a citywide multi-institution event focusing on Los Angeles themes (Los Angeles after World War II in 2011-2012, and Latin American art in 2017).<ref name=":0" /><ref>Noriega, Chon. [https://www.chicano.ucla.edu/about/news/deborah-marrow-presente "Deborah Marrow, Presente!"] UCLA Chicano Studies Research Center.</ref> She also took an interest in funding digitization projects at major museums, through the Online Scholarly Catalogue Initiative (from 2008 to 2014),<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=https://www.artnews.com/art-news/news/deborah-marrow-dead-13322/|title=Deborah Marrow, Game-Changing Getty Foundation Leader, Has Died|last=Greenberger|first=Alex|date=2019-10-02|website=ARTnews.com|language=en-US|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref><ref name=":1" /> and set up a $2 million fund for historical preservation in New Orleans after [[Hurricane Katrina]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://philanthropynewsdigest.org/newsmakers/deborah-marrow-director-getty-foundation-shaping-the-future-of-arts-grantmaking|title=Deborah Marrow, Director, Getty Foundation: Shaping the Future of Arts Grantmaking|last=Robbins|first=Emily|date=March 12, 2008|website=Philanthropy News Digest (PND)|language=en|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref>

Marrow served as interim president of the J. Paul Getty Trust twice, in 2006-2007 and in 2010-2011;<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.ocregister.com/2010/06/15/getty-appoints-interim-president-and-ceo/|title=Getty appoints interim president and CEO|date=2010-06-15|website=Orange County Register|language=en-US|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref> at the time of her death, she was the only woman to ever serve as president of the Trust. She retired from the Trust in 2018, and the Getty Multicultural Undergraduate Internship was renamed the Getty Marrow Multicultural Undergraduate Internship Program in her honor that year.<ref name=":2" />

Marrow also served on the board of trustees the University of Pennsylvania for sixteen years, from 2003 to 2019.<ref name=":4" />

== Personal life ==
Deborah Marrow married engineer Michael J. McGuire in 1971.<ref name=":3" /> They had two children, Anna and David. She died in 2019, aged 70, in [[Santa Monica, California]].<ref name=":0" /> <ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/story/2019-10-04/deborah-marrow-getty-foundation-director-dies|title=Deborah Marrow, longtime Getty Foundation director and arts funder, dies|date=2019-10-04|website=Los Angeles Times|language=en-US|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref>
