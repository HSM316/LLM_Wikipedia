The '''Church of St James the Less''' is an active [[Church of England]] parish church in [[Hambridge and Westport|Hambridge]], [[Somerset]], England.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.achurchnearyou.com/church/11265/ |title=St James the Less |publisher=A Church Near You |access-date=2019-12-31}}</ref> It was designed by [[Benjamin Ferrey]] and built in 1842–44. The church has been a [[Listed building|Grade II listed building]] since 1988.<ref name="historicengland1">{{cite web|url=https://historicengland.org.uk/listing/the-list/list-entry/1263773 |title=CHURCH OF ST JAMES THE LESS, Hambridge and Westport - 1263773 |publisher=Historic England |access-date=2019-12-31}}</ref> Adjacent the church is a former national school, which was built in 1844 and is also Grade II listed.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=SCPNAAAAMAAJ&q=hambridge+%22national+school%22+1844&pg=PA223 |title=Kelly's Directory of Somersetshire: With the City of Bristol - Google Books |year=1883 |access-date=2019-12-31}}</ref>

==History==
The Church of St James the Less was built as the district church for Hambridge and Westport. Prior to its construction, inhabitants were between two and three and a half miles from the parish church of [[St Andrew's Church, Curry Rivel|St Andrew]] at [[Curry Rivel]].<ref>{{cite news |title=Taunton.|url=https://www.britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000348/18420615/034/0007 |newspaper=The Taunton Courier |date=15 June 1842|access-date=31 December 2019|via = [[British Newspaper Archive]] |url-access=subscription }}</ref>

The plans for the church were drawn up by [[Benjamin Ferrey]] of [[London]] and Mr. Maurice Davis of [[Langport]] was hired as the builder.<ref name="britishnewspaperarchive1">{{cite news |title=Langport.|url=https://www.britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000348/18440214/025/0007 |newspaper=The Taunton Courier |date=14 February 1844|access-date=31 December 2019|via = [[British Newspaper Archive]] |url-access=subscription }}</ref> A plot of land was purchased for £140 and the church built for an approximate cost of £800.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://images.lambethpalacelibrary.org.uk/luna/servlet/detail/LPLIBLPL~12~12~176333~125839 |title=Church of England Record Centre (CERC) |publisher=Images.lambethpalacelibrary.org.uk |access-date=2019-12-31}}</ref> A grant of £120 was received from the Bath and Wells Diocesan Church Building Association.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=_psOAAAAQAAJ&q=Hambridge+church&pg=PA79 |title=The Ecclesiastical gazette, or, Monthly register of the affairs of the ... - Google Books |year=1843 |access-date=2019-12-31}}</ref>

The foundation stone was laid on 20 June 1842,<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=OdBPAAAAMAAJ&q=%22parish%22+hambridge+Westport&pg=PA117 |title=The British Magazine and Monthly Register of Religious and Ecclesiastical ... - Google Books |year=1842 |access-date=2019-12-31}}</ref> and the church and its cemetery were consecrated by the [[Bishop of Salisbury]], the Right Rev. [[Edward Denison (bishop)|Edward Denison]], on 2 February 1844.<ref name="britishnewspaperarchive1"/>

The church underwent a £1,500 restoration scheme in 1957–58, after a survey of the building identified essential repairs to be made. A bazaar held in October 1958 was successful in clearing the remaining £144 debt.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://somerset-cat.swheritage.org.uk/records/D/D/cf/1957/9/4 |title=Faculty papers for Hambridge |publisher=Somerset-cat.swheritage.org.uk |access-date=2019-12-31}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |title=Restoration of parish church|url=https://www.britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000528/19581018/137/0011 |newspaper=The Taunton Courier |date=18 October 1958|access-date=31 December 2019|via = [[British Newspaper Archive]] |url-access=subscription }}</ref>

==Architecture==
St James is built of [[White lias]], with [[Ham stone]] dressings and slate roofs, in the [[Decorated style]]. It is made up of a five-[[bay (architecture)|bay]] [[nave]], [[chancel]], west tower, north organ chamber, south vestry and south porch. The two-stage tower has battlements, pinnacles, gargoyles and a diagonal buttress on the west side. It contains a single bell.<ref name="historicengland1"/> The church was designed to hold 246 sittings, 150 of which were free and unappropriated.<ref name="britishnewspaperarchive1"/>

A stained glass window was added to the church in 1866 in memory of the fur dyer and engineer [[John Appold]]. It was made by O'Connor and paid for by Rev. C. S. Grueber and friends.<ref>{{cite news |title=Langport.|url=https://www.britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000406/18661109/037/0006 |newspaper=The Western Gazette |date=9 November 1866|access-date=31 December 2019|via = [[British Newspaper Archive]] |url-access=subscription }}</ref>
