'''Estrogenic substances''', sold under the brand name '''Amniotin''' among others, is an [[estrogen (medication)|estrogen]] medication which was marketed in the 1930s and 1940s and is no longer available.<ref name="JAMA1943">{{cite journal|title=Nomenclature of Endocrine Preparations|journal=Journal of the American Medical Association|volume=123|issue=6|year=1943|pages=351|issn=0002-9955|doi=10.1001/jama.1943.02840410033009}}</ref><ref name="pmid18744783">{{cite journal | vauthors = Fluhmann CF | title = Estrogenic Hormones: Their Clinical Usage | journal = Cal West Med | volume = 49 | issue = 5 | pages = 362–6 | date = November 1938 | pmid = 18744783 | pmc = 1659459 | doi = | url = }}</ref><ref name="Greene1941">{{cite journal|last1=Greene|first1=R.R.|title=Endocrine Therapy for Gynecologic Disorders|journal=Medical Clinics of North America|volume=25|issue=1|year=1941|pages=155–168|issn=00257125|doi=10.1016/S0025-7125(16)36624-X}}</ref><ref name="Fluhmann1944">{{cite journal|last1=Fluhmann|first1=C. F.|title=Clinical use of extracts from the ovaries|journal=Journal of the American Medical Association|volume=125|issue=1|year=1944|pages=1|issn=0002-9955|doi=10.1001/jama.1944.02850190003001}}</ref><ref name="Reifenstein1944">{{cite journal|last1=Reifenstein|first1=Edward C.|title=Endocrinology: A Synopsis of Normal and Pathologic Physiology, Diagnostic Procedures, and Therapy|journal=Medical Clinics of North America|volume=28|issue=5|year=1944|pages=1232–1276|issn=00257125|doi=10.1016/S0025-7125(16)36180-6}}</ref> It was a purified [[extract]] of [[animal material]] such as [[horse]] [[urine]], [[placenta]], and/or [[amniotic fluid]], and contained a non-[[crystalline]] [[mixture]] of estrogens, including [[estrone (medication)|estrone]], [[estradiol (medication)|17β-estradiol]], [[alfatradiol|17α-estradiol]], and/or [[equilin]].<ref name="Greene1941" /><ref name="JAMA1943" /><ref name="Barr1940" /><ref name="AMA1935" /><ref name="Reifenstein1944" /><ref name="McCullagh1935">{{cite journal | vauthors = McCullagh EP | title = The Management of Functional Menstrual Disorders | journal = Cleveland Clinic Quarterly | year = 1935 | volume = 2 | issue = 4 | pages = 52–64 | issn = | pmid = | doi = | url = }}</ref> The medication was thought to contain estrone as its major active ingredient<ref name="Barr1940">{{cite book|author=David Preswick Barr|title=Modern Medical Therapy in General Practice|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=V5HJymGfFP8C|year=1940|publisher=William & Wilkins Company|quote=Complex Estrogenic Preparations. 1. Amniotin (Squibb). This is a highly purified but not crystalline preparation derived from pregnant mares' urine. The chief active ingredient is apparently ketohydroxyestrin (estrone).}}</ref> and was described as an estrone-like preparation, or as "essentially estrone".<ref name="JAMA1943" /><ref name="Greene1941" /><ref name="Reifenstein1944" /> Estrogenic substances was originally produced from the urine of pregnant women, placenta, and/or amniotic fluid, but by the early 1940s, it was manufactured exclusively from the urine of [[stallion]]s or [[pregnancy|pregnant]] [[mare]]s, similarly to almost all other estrogen preparations on the market.<ref name="AMA1935">{{cite book|title=Glandular Physiology and Therapy|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=HKlFAAAAYAAJ|year=1935|publisher=American Medical Association|page=480|quote=Amniotin, E. R. Squibb & Sons: This is an estrogenic preparation originally derived from amniotic fluid; it is not reduced to the crystalline state during manufacture. More recently, according to the firm, the urine of pregnant mares has served as an added source of active material.}}</ref><ref name="McCullagh1935" /><ref name="Barr1940" /><ref name="JAMA1943" />

Estrogenic substances was marketed under a variety of different brand names including ''Amniotin'' ([[Bristol-Myers Squibb|Squibb]]), ''Estrogenic Hormones'' ([[Upjohn]], others), ''Estrogenic Substances'' (Reed and Carnrick, others), ''Estrolin'' (Lakeside), ''Estromone'', ''Folestrin'' ([[Armour and Company|Armour]]), ''Follacro'' (Schieffelin), ''Menformon'' ([[Hoffmann-La Roche|Roche]]-[[Organon International|Organon]]), ''Oestroform'', ''Ova-Estrin'', and ''Theelestrin'', among others.<ref name="JAMA1943" /><ref name="pmid18744783" /><ref name="Greene1941" /><ref name="Fluhmann1944" /> It was provided in various [[pharmaceutical form|form]]s and [[route of administration|routes of administration]] including [[oil solution]] for [[intramuscular injection]], [[oral administration|oral]] [[tablet (pharmacy)|tablet]]s and [[capsule (pharmacy)|capsule]]s, [[vaginal administration|vaginal]] [[suppository|suppositories]], and [[transdermal administration|transdermal]] [[ointment]]s.<ref name="pmid18744783" /><ref name="Greene1941" /><ref name="Fluhmann1944" /><ref name="JAMA1943" />

Estrogen medications similar to but distinct from estrogenic substances included [[conjugated estriol]] (''Emmenin'') and [[conjugated estrogens]] (''Premarin'').<ref name="JAMA1943" /> They are also non-crystalline mixtures of estrogens.<ref name="JAMA1943" /> Estrogenic substances were also distinct from pure crystalline preparations such as [[estrone (medication)|estrone]], [[estradiol (medication)|estradiol]], [[estriol (medication)|estriol]], [[estradiol benzoate]], and [[estradiol dipropionate]].<ref name="JAMA1943" /> The medication should additionally be distinguished from estrogen [[ovary|ovarian]] extracts, which had little activity and were generally considered to be essentially inactive.<ref name="Novak1935">{{cite journal|last1=Novak|first1=Emil|title=The Therapeutic Use of Estrogenic Substances|journal=JAMA: The Journal of the American Medical Association|volume=104|issue=20|year=1935|pages=1815|issn=0098-7484|doi=10.1001/jama.1935.92760200002012}}</ref><ref name="pmid18744783" />
