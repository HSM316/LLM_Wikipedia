'''Leah C. Stokes''' is a political science communicator and Professor of Political Science at the [[University of California, Santa Barbara]]. Stokes investigates the politics around energy and environmental policy in the [[United States]]. She regularly writes about energy policy for newspapers and on social media.

== Early life and education ==
Stokes earned her undergraduate degree in psychology and [[East Asia|East Asian]] studies at the [[University of Toronto]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://kleinmanenergy.upenn.edu/visiting-scholar/leah-stokes|title=Leah Stokes|date=2019-09-19|website=kleinmanenergy.upenn.edu|language=en|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref> She completed a [[Master of Public Administration]] at [[Columbia University]]. After graduating Stokes worked with [[Bruce Stanton]] at the [[Parliament of Canada]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://epic.uchicago.edu/people/leah-c-stokes/|title=Leah C. Stokes|website=EPIC|language=en-US|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref> Her role involved policy analysis for the Environment and Sustainable Development Committee. In 2010 Stokes moved to [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology]] as graduate student, where she earned a Master's degree and a doctorate under the supervision [[Lawrence Susskind]]. At [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology]] Stokes created an environmental policy curriculum, including ''The Mercury Game'', a treaty negotiation that is currently used by over 100 universities around the world.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://mercurygame.scripts.mit.edu/game/|title=The Mercury Game|website=mercurygame.scripts.mit.edu|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref> 

== Career ==
In 2015 Stokes joined the faculty at the [[University of California, Santa Barbara]]. Her research considers energy policy and environmental policy in the [[United States]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://climatecommunication.yale.edu/about/people-partners/leah-stokes/|title=Leah Stokes|website=Yale Program on Climate Change Communication|language=en-US|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref> She has investigated the interaction between public opinion and policy making around [[renewable energy]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=http://news.mit.edu/2017/bolstering-public-support-for-state-level-renewable-energy-policies-0630|title=Bolstering public support for state-level renewable energy policies|website=MIT News|access-date=2019-12-30}}</ref> She looked at what determines public opinion in particular states, and how the design and presentation of [[Renewable portfolio standards in the United States|Renewable Portfolio Standards]] (RPS) changes public support for a particular policy. After conducting a survey of almost 60,000 people, Stokes identified that politicians are responsive to public opinion.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Stokes|first=Leah C.|last2=Warshaw|first2=Christopher|date=2017-06-30|title=Renewable energy policy design and framing influence public support in the United States|url=https://www.nature.com/articles/nenergy2017107|journal=Nature Energy|language=en|volume=2|issue=8|pages=1–6|doi=10.1038/nenergy.2017.107|issn=2058-7546}}</ref>
