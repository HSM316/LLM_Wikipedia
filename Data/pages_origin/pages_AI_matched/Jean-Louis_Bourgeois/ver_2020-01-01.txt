'''Jean-Louis Bourgeois''' (born 1940) is an author and the son of artist [[Louise Bourgeois]] and art historian [[Robert Goldwater]]. Bourgeois studied literature and architectural history   at [[Harvard University]].
In 1969 and 1970 Bourgeois worked at [[ArtForum]] before becoming interested in the production and history of mud brick architecture.<ref name=walrus2004>Alex Ulam.  [http://www.goodwork7.org/walrus.pdf "The Clown. How to tell jokes that win friends and influence people in an ancient city in sub-Saharan Africa]  The Walrus. July/August 2004. pp.24-5 Bourgeois intends to translate the article into Funny. </ref> He is the author of the volume "Spectacular Vernacular: the Adobe Tradition" (with photographs taken by Carollee Pelos)<ref>Jean-Louis Bourgeois, Carollee Pelos. Spectacular Vernacular: a New Appreciation of Traditional Desert Architecture. Peregrine Smith Books, 1983. {{ISBN|9780879051440}}</ref> which established him as an expert on the subject.<ref>[https://www.nytimes.com/2006/10/08/realestate/08deal1.html?_r=1 7 Stories of Water in a 2-Story Building] by Josh Barbanel, The New York Times. Published: October 8, 2006.</ref>  He owns a home in [[Djenne]], [[Mali]] and has actively been involved in architectural conservation efforts there including the preservation of the world's largest adobe building the [[Great Mosque of Djenne]], <ref>Deidre d'Entremont. [http://www.culturalsurvival.org/publications/cultural-survival-quarterly/mali/djenne-project-mali-jean-louis-bourgeois-coordinator The Djenne Project, Mali: Jean Louis Bourgeois, Coordinator.] Cultural Survival Quarterly  25.2 (Summer 2001) Endangered Languages, Endangered Lives.</ref> and has written extensively on the subject <ref>Jean-Louis Bourgeois. [https://www.jstor.org/pss/3336477?cookieSet=1 The History of the Great Mosques of Djenné]. African Arts Vol. 20, No. 3 (May, 1987), pp. 54-63+90-92</ref> While living in Djennê, Bourgeois opposed the Talo Dam project, and became a fixture in the city's cultural life.<ref name=walrus2004 />  He appeared in the biopic on his mother [[Louise Bourgeois: The Spider, the Mistress, and the Tangerine]].<ref> In " Louise Bourgeois: The Spider, the Mistress, and the Tangerine"   [https://www.imdb.com/title/tt1386654/]</ref>  Bourgeois owns an adobe house in [[Taos, New Mexico]] and has written on the Southwestern American Indian Adobe tradition <ref>[[Ianto Evans]], Michael G. [[File:393-393 West Street 6 Weehawken Street from north.jpg|thumb|393-393 West Street 6 Weehawken Street from north]]

In December, 2016 Jean-Louis Bourgeois announced he was giving his $4 million historical house in New York City to the Ramapough Lenape native americans who intend to use it as a meeting house.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Smith-Slogan|first1=Christy|title=Millionaire returning $4M piece of Manhattan to Indian Tribe|url=https://nypost.com/2016/12/18/millionaire-returning-4m-trophy-house-to-indian-tribe/|accessdate=22 December 2016|publisher=New York Post|date=December 18, 2016}}</ref> The building, located at 6 Weehawken Street in the West Village (also known as 392-393 West Street) was formerly a historical public market. The deed has been transferred to a non-profit organization run by the Lenape tribe, who were among the original inhabitants of Manhattan.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Cohen|first1=Michelle|title=Louise Bourgeois’ son gifts $4M West Village townhouse to Native American tribe|url=https://www.6sqft.com/louise-bourgeois-son-gifts-4m-west-village-townhouse-to-native-american-tribe/|website=6sqft.com|accessdate=22 December 2016}}</ref>
