'''Ryan André Brasseaux''' (born 1976) is an American [[scholar]] of vernacular American [[music]] and an education administrator. He has served as the Dean of [[Davenport College]] at [[Yale University]] since 2011.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|last=Griswold|first=Alison|date=2011-08-05|title=Ryan André Brasseaux GRD '08 picked as Davenport dean|url=https://yaledailynews.com/blog/2011/08/05/ryan-andre-brasseaux-grd-08-picked-as-davenport-dean/|website=Yale Daily News}}</ref> He is an expert on the history of Cajun music, and a frequent collaborator with the [[Lost Bayou Ramblers]].

==Early life and education==
Ryan André Brasseaux was born in 1976; his father is Cajun historian [[Carl A. Brasseaux]].<ref>{{Cite web|date=9 June 2002|title=Brasseaux Lectures At Yale|url=http://www.newspapers.com/image/229159553/|url-access=subscription|access-date=2021-05-15|website=Newspapers.com|publisher=Daily World|page=22|language=en}}</ref>

He did undergraduate work in anthropology at the [[University of Louisiana at Lafayette]] and earned an M.A. degree from [[Louisiana State University]].{{When|date=May 2021}} He received a M.A. degree and [[Master of Philosophy|M. Phil.]] degree in 2008 and his Ph.D. in 2011 from Yale University.<ref name="DC Dean Profile">{{cite web|title=Dean Ryan Brasseaux|url=http://davenport.yalecollege.yale.edu/people/dean-ryan-brasseaux|publisher=Yale University|accessdate=June 2, 2014}}</ref> He was awarded a Beinecke Research Fellowship, the Lamar Research Scholar, Kinney/Tesoro Fellowships from the Howard R. Lamar Center for the Study of Frontiers and Borders, and a Prize Teaching Fellowship for excellence in undergraduate instruction and mentorship.<ref>http://www.yale.edu/amstud/gradstudents.html; http://etd.lsu.edu/docs/available/etd-11102004-152234/</ref>

==Work==
In 2007, Brasseaux helped found Public Humanities at Yale University, a graduate student initiative that evolved into an ongoing lecture series and a Masters of Arts program.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Welcome &#124; American Studies|url=http://www.yale.edu/amstud/publichumanities/}}</ref>

Brasseaux has presented his work across the [[United States]], including invited lectures in [[New York City]], [[Yale University]], to the Fulbright Institute, and as the keynote speaker at [[Nashville, Tennessee|Nashville]]'s International Country Music Conference.<ref>[http://www.internationalcountrymusic.org/schedule.html International Country Music Conference – Schedule<!-- Bot generated title -->]</ref> He has also lectured extensively, accompanied by [[Grammy]]-nominated Cajun band the [[Lost Bayou Ramblers]], on the origins and evolution of Cajun music.<ref>[http://www.lsue.edu/acadgate/music/lostbayouramblers.htm The Lost Bayou Ramblers<!-- Bot generated title -->] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140201181748/http://www.lsue.edu/acadgate/music/lostbayouramblers.htm |date=2014-02-01 }}</ref>

In the wake of [[Hurricane Katrina]] in 2005, Brasseaux worked as research associate for the nationally syndicated public radio program ''[[American Routes]]'' ([[KRVS-FM|KRVS]] 88.7 [[FM Band|FM]]) hosted by Nick Spitzer.<ref>{{Cite news|last=Freedman|first=Samuel G.|date=2005-09-07|title=This Song Goes Out to You, Big Easy|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2005/09/07/arts/music/this-song-goes-out-to-you-big-easy.html|access-date=2021-05-15|issn=0362-4331|quote=He hired a local cultural historian who is also the host of a show on KRVS, Ryan Brasseau, to find music and research previous natural disasters, from the devastating Mississippi River flood of 1927 to Hurricane Betsy in 1965.}}</ref>

==Publications==
