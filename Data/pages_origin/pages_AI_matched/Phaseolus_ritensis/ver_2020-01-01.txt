'''''Phaseolus ritensis''''' is a plant [[species]] native to [[Arizona]], [[Sonora]], [[Chihuahua (state)|Chihuahua]], [[Sinaloa]] and [[Nuevo León]]. Common names include '''"[[Santa Rita Mountains|Santa Rita Mountain]] bean"''' (in US) and '''"[[cocolmeca]]"''' in Mexico. It grows in forested areas in the mountains.

''Phaseolus ritensis'' is a perennial herb with a large woody [[taproot]]. It is a trailing herb with [[trifoliate]] leaves and pink to lavender flowers.<ref name="jkl">M.E.Jones, Contributions of Western Botany. 12: 14. 1908</ref><ref>Thomas H. Kearney & Robert H. Peebles. 1979. Arizona Flora, with Supplement, Second Edition. University of California Press, Berkeley./ {{ISBN|0-520-00637-2}}</ref><ref>P. Martin et al. 2000. Gentry's Rio Mayo Plants. The Tropical Deciduous Forest & Environs of Northwest Mexico. University of Arizona Press, Tucson.</ref><ref>Marechal, R., J. M. Mascherpa & F. Stainier. 1978. Etude taxonomique d'un groupe complexe d'especes ... ''Phaseolus'' et ''Vigna'' (Papilionaceae) sur ... informatique. Boissiera 28: 1–273.</ref>

==Uses==
The plant is widely valued for both food and medicine throughout much of its native range.{{cn|date=January 2019}} Green and ripe fruits served as an important food source in times past. Roots are the source of medicine, glue, and a fermenting agent.<ref>G.P Nabhan, J.W. Berry & C.W. Weber. Wild beans of the greater Southwest: ''Phaseolus metcalfei'' and ''P. ritensis''. Economic Botany 34:68-85. 1980.</ref><ref>Pennington, CW. 1963. The Tarahumar of Mexico, their material culture. University of Utah Press, Salt Lake City</ref>
