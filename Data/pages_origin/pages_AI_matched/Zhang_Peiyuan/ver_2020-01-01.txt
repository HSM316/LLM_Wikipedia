'''Zhang Peiyuan''' (died 1934) was a [[Han chinese]] general, commander of the Ili garrison. He fought against Uighur and Tungans during the Kumul revolt, but then secretly negotiated with the Tungan general, [[Ma Zhongying]] to form an alliance against [[Sheng Shicai]] and the Soviet Union.<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=XeBxAAAAMAAJ|title=Under the Soviet shadow: the Yining Incident : ethnic conflicts and international rivalry in Xinjiang, 1944–1949|author=David D. Wang|year=1999|publisher=The Chinese University Press|location=Hong Kong|page=52|isbn=962-201-831-9}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=P3xCAAAAYAAJ|title=Xinjiang: the land and the people|author=Pʻing Cheng|year=1989|publisher=New World Press|page=54|isbn=7-80005-078-5}}</ref> Zhang's army had about 3,000 soldiers.<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=r3AJFusMHJwC&pg=PA122|title=Biographical dictionary of Republican China, Volume 3|author1=Howard L. Boorman |author2=Richard C. Howard |author3=Joseph K. H. Cheng |year=1970|publisher=Columbia University Press|isbn=0-231-08957-0|page=122}}</ref> They almost destroyed Sheng's armies when the Soviet Union [[Soviet Invasion of Xinjiang|invaded Xinjiang]] and overran Zhang's forces.<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=8FVsWq31MtMC&pg=PA196|title=Eurasian crossroads: a history of Xinjiang|author=James A. Millward|year=2007|publisher=Columbia University Press|location=New York City|page=199|isbn=978-0-231-13924-3}}</ref> Zhang committed suicide to avoid capture by the Soviets at Muzart Pass during a snow storm.<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=IAs9AAAAIAAJ|title=Warlords and Muslims in Chinese Central Asia: a political history of Republican Sinkiang 1911–1949|author=Andrew D. Forbes|year=1986|publisher=CUP Archive|location=Cambridge, England|isbn=0-521-25514-7|pages=120, 238}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=XeBxAAAAMAAJ|title=Under the Soviet shadow: the Yining Incident : ethnic conflicts and international rivalry in Xinjiang, 1944-1949|author=David D.
