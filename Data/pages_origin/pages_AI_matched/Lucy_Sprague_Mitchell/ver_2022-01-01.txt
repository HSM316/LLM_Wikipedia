'''Lucy Sprague Mitchell''' (July 2, 1878 – October 15, 1967<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.oxfordreference.com/view/10.1093/acref/9780199754663.001.0001/acref-9780199754663|title=Dictionary of Modern American Philosophers - Oxford Reference|year=2005|language=en|doi=10.1093/acref/9780199754663.001.0001|isbn=9780199754663|access-date=2018-02-15}}</ref>) was an American educator and children's writer, and the founder of [[Bank Street College of Education]].<ref>{{cite news|last1=Chesler|first1=Ellen|title=She Wanted it All, and Got It|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1987/03/22/books/she-wanted-it-all-and-got-it.html|access-date=March 7, 2015|work=New York Times|date=March 22, 1987}}</ref>

== Early life and education ==
Lucy Sprague was born in [[Chicago]], Illinois, the daughter of Otho A. S. Sprague and Lucia Atwood Sprague. Her father was a businessman.<ref name=":1">{{Cite news|date=1909-05-09|title=Women Inherit Half Million Each|pages=24|work=Oakland Tribune|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84350058/women-inherit-half-million-each/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> She attended Radcliffe College from 1896 to 1900, graduating with honors in philosophy.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Haller|first=Evelyn|date=1988|title=Lucy Sprague Mitchell: The Making of a Modern Woman (review)|url=https://muse.jhu.edu/article/372206/pdf|journal=Biography|volume=11|issue=4|pages=331–336|doi=10.1353/bio.2010.0616|s2cid=161651043|via=Project MUSE}}</ref> During her time at [[Radcliffe College]], Mitchell lived with [[Alice Freeman Palmer]] and [[George Herbert Palmer]] on Quincy Street in Cambridge, Massachusetts. Because of the college's strict codes of gender segregation at the time, Mitchell had to circumvent the all-male [[Harvard Yard]] in order to reach [[Harvard University]]'s [[Museum of Comparative Zoology]], where she worked in the Radcliffe Zoological Laboratory.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Tonn|first=Jenna|date=2017|title=Extralaboratory Life: Gender Politics and Experimental Biology at Radcliffe College, 1894–1910|journal=Gender & History|volume=29|issue=2|pages=329–358|doi=10.1111/1468-0424.12292}}</ref>

Her sister Mary married scientist [[Adolph C. Miller]].<ref name=":1" /> Pianist [[Elizabeth Sprague Coolidge]] was her first cousin.<ref>{{Cite news|last=Cass|first=Judith|date=1944-10-27|title=Music to Mark 80th Year of Ex-Chicagoan|pages=23|work=Chicago Tribune|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84350498/music-to-mark-80th-year-of/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref>

== Career ==
Mitchell was the first dean of women at the [[University of California, Berkeley|University of California at Berkeley]],<ref name=":0">{{Cite news|date=1967-10-17|title=U.C.'s First Women's Dean Dies|pages=33|work=Oakland Tribune|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84348666/ucs-first-womens-dean-dies/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> where she lectured in the English Department and promoted educational and career opportunities for women students from 1903 to 1912.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Ruyle|first1=Janet|title=Dean Lucy Sprague, the Partheneia, and the Arts|url=http://www.cshe.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/shared/publications/docs/chron2_excerpt_Partheneia.pdf|publisher=Chronicle of the University of California|access-date=March 8, 2015}}</ref> She was succeeded by her assistant, fellow Radcliffe alumna [[Lucy Ward Stebbins]].<ref name=":2" /> In 1916, influenced by the work of [[John Dewey]], Mitchell cofounded the Bureau of Educational Experiments (BEE) in New York City to study and develop optimal learning environments for children.<ref>{{cite web|title=A Brief History|url=http://www.bankstreet.edu/discover-bankstreet/what-we-do/history/|website=Bank Street College of Education|access-date=March 8, 2015}}</ref><ref>Winsor, Charlotte B.; Stodt, Martha (1957). [https://educate.bankstreet.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1005&context=books "Teacher Education for a Changing World"] (PDF). ''Bank Street Profile: An Informal Report: 1916-1956.''</ref> The BEE evolved into the Bank Street College of Education.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Nager|first1=Nancy|last2=Shapiro|first2=Edith|date=2007|title=A Progressive Approach to the Education of Teachers: Some Principles from Bank Street College of Education|url=https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED495462.pdf|journal=Occasional Paper Series|volume=18}}</ref>

Mitchell wrote over twenty books,<ref name=":0" /> including ''North America'' (1931),<ref>{{Cite news|date=1931-07-18|title=Van Doren Terms Junior Guild Book Excellent Western Tale|pages=11|work=Lansing State Journal|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84348214/van-doren-terms-junior-guild-book/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> ''Streets: Stories for Children Under Seven'' (1933), ''Horses Now and Long Ago'' (1938), ''The Here and Now Story Book'' (1938),<ref>{{Cite news|last=Bevans|first=Gladys|date=1938-10-31|title=Young Children Need Story Books About the Everyday World|pages=360|work=Daily News|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84347569/young-children-need-story-books-about/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> ''See What's in the Grass'' (1945),<ref>{{Cite news|date=1945-10-27|title=Children's Books|pages=12|work=The Kansas City Star|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84348434/childrens-books/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> ''Our Children and Our Schools'' (1950),<ref>{{Cite news|last=Sessler|first=Betty|date=1950-09-17|title=Old, New Education Ideas Compared|pages=67|work=The Times Dispatch|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84347379/old-new-education-ideas-comparedbetty/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> and ''Believe and Make Believe'' (1956).<ref>{{Cite news|last=Kinnaird|first=Clark|date=1956-11-17|title=Gifts for Children|pages=4|work=Pittsburgh Sun-Telegraph|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84348124/gifts-for-childrenclark-kinnaird/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> She also wrote a memoir of her marriage, ''Two Lives: The Story of Wesley Clair Mitchell and Myself'' (1953).<ref>{{Cite news|date=1953-07-04|title=Genius Can Be Happy|pages=2|work=The Indianapolis News|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84347953/genius-can-be-happy/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref>

== Personal life ==
Lucy Sprague married economist [[Wesley Clair Mitchell]] in 1912.<ref>{{Cite news|date=1912-05-07|title=Untitled social item|pages=8|work=The San Francisco Call|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/64195822/the-san-francisco-call/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref><ref name=":2">{{Cite news|date=1912-04-11|title=Miss Stebbins to be New Dean|pages=10|work=Oakland Tribune|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84349932/miss-stebbins-to-be-new-dean/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> They raised four children together, including social scientist [[Arnold Mitchell]]. W. C. Mitchell died in 1948.<ref>{{Cite news|date=1948-10-31|title=Dr. Mitchell Dies in East|pages=21|work=The Decatur Daily Review|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84349292/dr-mitchell-dies-in-east/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> Lucy Sprague Mitchell died in 1967, aged 89 years, in [[Palo Alto, California]].<ref name=":0" /> [[Columbia University]] holds a collection of her papers.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Lucy Sprague Mitchell papers, 1878-1967|url=https://findingaids.library.columbia.edu/ead/nnc-rb/ldpd_4079116|url-status=live|access-date=2021-08-29|website=Rare Book & Manuscript Library, Columbia University Libraries Finding Aids}}</ref> Joyce Antler published a book-length biography of Mitchell, ''Lucy Sprague Mitchell: The Making of a Modern Woman'' (Yale University Press 1988).<ref>{{Cite news|last=Bussey|first=Charles J.|date=1988-07-24|title=Lucy Sprague Mitchell was a 'pioneer supermom'|pages=48|work=The Park City Daily News|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/84347693/lucy-sprague-mitchell-was-a-pioneer/|access-date=2021-08-29|via=Newspapers.com}}</ref>
