'''David Beigelman''' (1887 – February 1945), also known as '''Dawid Bajgelman''' and '''Dawid Beigelman''',<ref>Lucjan Dobroszycki (Ed.). ''The Chronicle of the Lodz Ghetto, 1941-1944''. New Haven: Yale University Press, 1984, Index to the English edition.</ref> was a Polish [[violin]]ist, [[orchestra leader]], and [[composer]] of [[Yiddish theatre]] music and songs.

== Biography ==
He was born in [[Ostrowiec Świętokrzyski|Ostrowiec]], [[Congress Poland]]<ref name="leksikon">Zalmen Zylbercweig. Leksikon fun Yidish theater, Book one, column 161.</ref> to a musical family in [[Łódź]] where he composed and performed in Yiddish theatres at a young age.<ref name=Kramer99>{{cite book|first=Aaron |last=Kramer |editor=Saul Lishinsky |title=The Last Lullaby: Poetry from the Holocaust |year=1999 |publisher=Syracuse University Press |isbn=0-8156-0579-X |page=99}}</ref><ref name=Savethemusic>SaveTheMusic.com, [http://archives.savethemusic.com/bin/archives.cgi?q=bio&id=David+Beigelman David Beigelman]. Retrieved 2014-11-30.</ref> He became director of the Lodz Yiddish Theater in 1912.<ref name="leksikon" />

He wrote the music for [[Julius Adler (composer)|Julius Adler]]'s operettas ''Dos Skoytn-meydl'' and ''Di mume Gnendil'' and [[Jacob Waxman|Yankev Vaksman]]'s ''Di Sheyne Berta'', all of which were staged in [[Łódź]], and arranged the music for [[S. Ansky]]'s ''[[The Dybbuk]]''. In 1929 he was composer and music director for the Ararat Theater in Łódź.<ref name="leksikon" />

In 1940 he was forced to move to the [[Ghetto Litzmannstadt]] in Łódź, where he took part in the ghetto's cultural life as a conductor<ref>Susan Willoughby ''Art, Music, and Writings from the Holocaust'' 2003 p56 Excerpt from Gypsy Song by David Beigelman - "David Beigelman was a professional composer and musician in Poland before the war. He was sent to the Lodz ghetto where he was the musical director for the ghetto theater."</ref> – the ghetto's first symphony concert was performed under his direction on 1 March 1941 – and as a composer of orchestral works and songs.<ref name=Savethemusic /> Two well-known Beigelman songs that have survived and are performed to this day are ''Kinder yorn (the years of childhood)'' and ''Tsigaynerlid (Gypsy Song)'', dedicated to the [[Romani people|Romani]] living in the ghetto.<ref name=Savethemusic /> He wrote songs to lyrics by Isaiah Spiegel including ''Makh tsu di eygelekh (Close Your Little Eyes)'' and ''Nisht keyn rozhinkes, nisht keyn mandlen (No raisins, no almonds)''.<ref>Zemerl, [http://www.zemerl.com/cgi-bin/print.pl?title=Makh+Tsu+Di+Eygelekh Makh Tsu Di Eygelekh] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090511075146/http://www.zemerl.com/cgi-bin/print.pl?title=Makh+Tsu+Di+Eygelekh |date=2009-05-11 }}. Retrieved 2010-06-02.</ref> He also collaborated with [[Moishe Broderzon]] writing well-known songs such as ''Nisim, nisim'' and ''Yidn, Shmidn''. He also collaborated with Moyshe Nudelman, David Herman, and Yakov Rotbaum.<ref>Fater, Isaschar (1970). Jewish Music in Poland between the Two World Wars, pp 53-58</ref>

In 1944, Beigelman was deported to [[Auschwitz]], where he died in February 1945.<ref name=Kramer99 /><ref name=Savethemusic /><ref name="Kramer">{{cite book|last1=Kramer|first1=Aaron|authorlink1=Aaron Kramer|last2=Lishinsky|first2=Saul |title=The Last Lullaby: Poetry from the Holocaust|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=lP2qbsCsmV0C&q=David+Beigelman|year=1999|publisher=[[Syracuse University Press]]|isbn=978-0-8156-0579-9|page=99}}</ref>
