'''Cosmo Stafford Crawley''' (27 May 1904 – 10 February 1989) was an English first-class [[cricket]]er, [[Rackets (sport)|rackets]] and [[real tennis]] player.

==Early life and cricket==
Crawley was the eldest son of [[Arthur Stafford Crawley]], then a [[curate]] at [[St Luke's, Chelsea]].<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.britishnewspaperarchive.co.uk/viewer/bl/0000327/19300605/078/0007|title=Mr. C.S. Crawley to wed|work=[[Derby Telegraph|Derby Daily Telegraph]]|page=7|date=5 June 1930|access-date=27 May 2024|url-access=subscription|via=[[British Newspaper Archive]]}}</ref> He was educated at [[Harrow School]], where he played for the school cricket team against [[Eton College|Eton]] and [[Winchester College|Winchester]], with him heading the [[batting average (cricket)|averages]] in 1922.<ref name="OBIT">{{cite web|url=https://www.espncricinfo.com/wisdenalmanack/content/story/236396.html|title=Wisden – Obituaries in 1989|date=9 February 2006 |publisher=ESPNcricinfo|access-date=27 May 2024}}</ref> From there, he matriculated to [[Magdalen College, Oxford]].<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=L_ueJ4rFw38C|title=Oxford University Calendar|date=2 June 2024 |publisher=[[University of Oxford]]|page=528|language=en}}</ref> He played one [[first-class cricket]] match for [[Hampshire County Cricket Club|Hampshire]] ''against'' [[Oxford University Cricket Club|Oxford University]] at [[University_Parks#Cricket_ground|Oxford]] in 1923.<ref name="FCM">{{cite web|url=https://cricketarchive.com/Archive/Players/6/6541/First-Class_Matches.html|title=First-Class Matches played by Cosmo Crawley|publisher=CricketArchive|access-date=27 May 2024|url-access=subscription}}</ref> The following season, he played once ''for'' Oxford University against [[Middlesex County Cricket Club|Middlesex]], in addition to playing for the [[Free Foresters Cricket Club|Free Foresters]] ''against'' Oxford University.<ref name="FCM"/> In 1925, he made a further two appearance for Oxford University against [[Leicestershire County Cricket Club|Leicestershire]].<ref name="FCM"/> He would later make two further first-class appearances, playing for the [[Harlequins Cricket Club|Harlequins]] in 1927, and for Middlesex in 1929, with Oxford University the opponents in both matches; all six of his first-class appearances thus came either for or against Oxford University.<ref name="FCM"/> In his six matches at first-class level, Crawley scored 243 runs at an [[batting average (cricket)|average]] of 22.09, with one half century.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.espncricinfo.com/cricketers/cosmo-crawley-10868|title=Player profile: Cosmo Crawley|publisher=ESPNcricinfo|access-date=27 May 2024}}</ref>

==Rackets and real tennis==
Crawley played [[Rackets (sport)|rackets]] while at Harrow School, and both rackets and [[real tennis]] while studying at Oxford. He won the British amateur rackets doubles championship in 1936 and 1937 with J. C. F. Simpson, and in 1939 and 1946 with [[John Pawle]].<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.tennisandrackets.com/editable/downloads/RacketsResults/AmateurDoubles.pdf|title=Amateur Doubles Championship|publisher=Tennis & Rackets Association|access-date=6 April 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170407143621/https://www.tennisandrackets.com/editable/downloads/RacketsResults/AmateurDoubles.pdf|archive-date=7 April 2017|url-status=dead}}</ref> He and Pawle also won the 1939 amateur real tennis doubles, beating the 3rd and 4th [[Baron Aberdare|Lord Aberdares]], then aged 54 and 20, in the final.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/obituaries/1482162/Lord-Aberdare.html|title=Lord Aberdare|newspaper=[[The Daily Telegraph]]|location=[[London]]|date=28 Jan 2005|access-date=6 April 2017|url-access=subscription}}</ref> He also captained the English rackets team against the United States in 1947, in addition to winning the Canadian Championship doubles with Pawle.<ref name="TIMES">{{cite news|url=https://link.gale.com/apps/doc/IF0501761433/TTDA?u=wikipedia&sid=bookmark-TTDA&xid=a3896e68|title=C. S. Crawley|work=[[The Times]]|location=[[London]]|page=12|issue=63321|date=18 February 1989|access-date=27 May 2024|url-access=subscription|via=[[Gale (publisher)|Gale]]}}</ref> Writing in ''[[The Times]]'' after Crawley's death, [[Alec Douglas-Home]] noted that "In the doubles he was master of all the arts of this fastest of ball-games".<ref name="TIMES"/>

==Later life and death==
Crawley joined the insurance broking firm of C.T. Bowring & Co. in 1925 and was a director 1934–67 and a consultant to the firm 1967–73. He was an underwriting member of [[Lloyd's]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://acscricket.com/wp-content/uploads/Oxford-University-Cricketers-A-D-March-2023.pdf|title=Oxford University cricketers - A to D|publisher=[[The Association of Cricket Statisticians and Historians|ACS]]|location=[[Cardiff]]|page=57|access-date=27 May 2024}}</ref> At the outbreak of the [[Second World War]], he was given an emergency [[Commissioned officer|commission]] as a [[Second_lieutenant#United_Kingdom_and_other_Commonwealth_countries|second lieutenant]] in the [[Coldstream Guards]],<ref>{{London Gazette|issue=34698|date=30 September 1939|page=6649|supp=y}}</ref> serving until 1945. Crawley died at [[Westminster]] in February 1989. His brother, [[Aidan Crawley|Aidan]], was a first-class cricketer and politician.<ref name="OBIT"/> His cousin, [[Charles Crawley]], was also a first-class cricketer.
