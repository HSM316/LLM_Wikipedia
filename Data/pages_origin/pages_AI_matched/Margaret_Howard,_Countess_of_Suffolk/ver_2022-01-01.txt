'''Margaret Howard, Countess of Suffolk''' born '''Margaret Hyde Leiter''' (1879 – 5 March 1968) was an American-born heiress who married into the [[British aristocracy]] and was known as one of the "Dollar Princesses."

==Early life==
[[File:Countess Suffolk LCCN2014692707.jpg|thumb|right|The Countess of Suffolk, 1910.]]
Margaret Hyde "Daisy" Leiter was born in [[Chicago]] in 1879. She was the third daughter and youngest of four children born to Mary Theresa ([[née]] Carver) and [[Levi Leiter|Levi Ziegler Leiter]], the co-founder of Field and Leiter [[dry goods]] business, and later partner in the [[Marshall Fields]] retail empire.  Her three older siblings were Joseph Leiter, a [[Harvard University|Harvard]] graduate,<ref name="1932Estate">{{cite news |title=WIFE AND CHILDREN GET LEITER ESTATE; Will to Be Filed in Chicago To- day Disposes of Fortune That May Exceed $10,000,000. WIFE TO GET TRUST INCOME Son Thomas, 20, Inherits Racing Stable and Famous Library Started by Grandmother. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1932/04/21/100721448.pdf |access-date=24 July 2018 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=April 21, 1932 |language=en}}</ref> [[Mary Curzon, Baroness Curzon of Kedleston|Mary Leiter]], who married the British Conservative statesman [[George Curzon, 1st Marquess Curzon of Kedleston|George Curzon]], later 1st [[Marquess Curzon of Kedleston]],<ref name="1906Estate">{{cite news |title=LADY CURZON'S WILL. Value of the Estate $59,875 — Most of It Left to Her Husband. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1906/08/08/101793412.pdf |access-date=24 July 2018 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=August 8, 1906}}</ref> and Nancy Lathrop Carver Leiter, the sponsor of [[USS Illinois (BB-7)|USS ''Illinois'']] who married Colin Powys Campbell.<ref>{{cite journal|journal=Newark Daily Advocate|title=She is Back From India|location=Newark, Ohio|date=1900-09-19|page=3}}</ref>  On her father's side, she was of Swiss-German descent.<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.independent.co.uk/news/people/lady-alexandra-metcalfe-1595421.html |title=Lady Alexandra Metcalfe |first=Sarah |last=Bradford |date=9 August 1995 |newspaper=The Independent |access-date=6 January 2015 |location=London}}</ref><ref>{{cite book |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=OJmfAAAAMAAJ&q=Levi+Leiter |title=After the Victorians |first=A.N. |last=Wilson |year=2005 |publisher=Hutchinson |page=596|isbn=9780091794842 }}</ref>

In 1881, her family moved to [[Washington, D.C.]] and entered society there, while summering in [[Bar Harbor, Maine]].<ref name="1904Wedding"/> While in Washington, they lived in the former home of [[James G. Blaine]] on [[Dupont Circle]] for several years.<ref name="LadySuffolk">{{cite news| url=http://tucson.com/news/blogs/streetsmarts/street-smarts-neighborhood-road-named-for-lady-suffolk/article_6a25978c-a9a2-5e94-937a-d7bb1df5da89.html|title=Street Smarts: Neighborhood, road named for Lady Suffolk  | location=Tucson, Arizona | newspaper=Arizona Daily Star | first=David | last=Leighton | date=2014-10-20}}</ref>  Daisy and her two sisters attended [[finishing school]] in England.<ref name="Needham2016"/>

In 1898, Daisy was painted by the prominent American portrait artist [[John Singer Sargent]]. In 1994, the Hon. Mary Howard bequeathed the painting to [[Kenwood House|English Heritage, Kenwood]]<ref name="artuk">{{cite web |title=Daisy Leiter, Later Margaret Hyde, 19th Countess of Suffolk |url=https://artuk.org/discover/artworks/daisy-leiter-later-margaret-hyde-19th-countess-of-suffolk-191843 |website=artuk.org |publisher=[[Art UK]] |access-date=24 September 2019 |language=en}}</ref>  Also that year, it was announced that she was chosen by Governor [[John Riley Tanner]] to christen the battleship [[USS Illinois (BB-7)|USS ''Illinois'']].<ref name="1898Honor">{{cite news |title=Miss Daisy Leiter Honored. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1898/09/07/102567173.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=7 September 1898}}</ref> The report was incorrect and her sister older sister Nancy, was in fact, the honored.<ref name="1898Error">{{cite news |title=GOV. TANNER MAKES AN ERROR.; Miss Nannie Leiter as a Result Will Christen the Illinois. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1898/09/08/102076683.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=8 September 1898}}</ref>

==Personal life==
In 1903, while visiting her sister Mary in India for the [[Delhi Durbar]] celebrations,<ref name="npg.org"/> Daisy met the [[aide-de-camp]] of her brother-in-law, [[George Curzon, 1st Marquess Curzon of Kedleston|George]], who was [[Viceroy of India]].<ref>{{London Gazette|issue=27308|page=2861|date=26 April 1901}}</ref>  He was [[Henry Howard, 19th Earl of Suffolk|Henry Molyneux Paget Howard, 19th Earl of Suffolk, 12th Earl of Berkshire]], who was known to be short of money.<ref name="npg.org"/>  In July 1904, she was reportedly engaged to Major Crawley, but the engagement was called off.<ref name="1904EngagementCalledOff">{{cite news |title=Daisy Leiter's Engagement Broken? |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1904/07/26/117943854.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=26 July 1904}}</ref> They eventually became engaged and when ''[[The New York Times]]'' reported the engagement in December 1904, they noted that the Earl's "estate [[Charlton Park, Wiltshire|Charlton Park]]... in Wiltshire will be restored to the glory of its former days."<ref name="1904Engagement">{{cite news |title=DAISY LEITER ENGAGED TO EARL OF SUFFOLK; Mrs. Leiter Makes the Announcement in Chicago. WEDDING IN NEAR FUTURE Third of Late L. Z. Leiter's Daughters to Wed Englishman -- Engagement Rumored Before, but Denied. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1904/12/12/101404203.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=12 December 1904}}</ref>  Daisy and Lord Suffolk eventually wed on 26 December 1904, several months after the death of her father, at the Leiter family residence in Washington, D.C., with Daisy wearing  "a coronet of diamonds, part of the family jewels of the house of Suffolk and Berkshire, which was sent from England for the event."<ref name="1904Wedding">{{cite news |last1=Times |first1=Special to The New York |title=DAISY LEITER MARRIES; Bride of the Earl of Suffolk -- Will Go Abroad This Week. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1904/12/27/120273924.pdf |access-date=18 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=27 December 1904}}</ref>  Henry was the eldest son of [[Henry Howard, 18th Earl of Suffolk]].<ref name="EarlSuffolk">{{cite web |title=Suffolk, Earl of (E, 1603) |url=http://www.cracroftspeerage.co.uk/online/content/suffolk1603.htm |website=cracroftspeerage.co.uk |publisher=Heraldic Media Limited |access-date=18 September 2019 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140425124340/http://www.cracroftspeerage.co.uk/online/content/suffolk1603.htm |archive-date=25 April 2014 |url-status=dead }}</ref> Together, they were the parents of:<ref name="EarlSuffolk"/><ref>"Cracroft's Peerage: The Complete Guide to the British Peerage & Baronetage", [http://www.cracroftspeerage.co.uk/online/content/suffolk1603.htm "Suffolk, Earl of (E, 1603)"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140425124340/http://www.cracroftspeerage.co.uk/online/content/suffolk1603.htm |date=2014-04-25 }}, ''The Peerage Research Trust''. Retrieved 27 May 2014.</ref>

* The Hon. [[Charles Howard, 20th Earl of Suffolk|Charles Henry George Howard]] (1906–1941), who married American-born ballet dancer, Mimi Forde-Piggott, known as "Mimi Crawford", daughter of Alfred George Forde-Piggott and niece of [[Robert Chalmers, 1st Baron Chalmers]], the former [[Governor-General of Ceylon]].<ref name="1934Engagement">{{cite news |title=BRITISH PEER TO WED MISS MIMI CRAWFORD; The Earl of Suffolk, Kin of Late Chicago Merchant, Engaged to English Dancer. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1934/02/22/95035090.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=22 February 1934}}</ref>
* The Hon. Cecil John Arthur Howard (1908–1985), who married [[Frances Drake|Frances Drake Dean]], daughter of Edwin Morgan Dean,<ref name="1939Wedding">{{cite news |title=CECIL J.A. HOWARD WEDS; Frances Drake Is Married to Brother of Earl of Suffolk |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1939/02/14/95758732.pdf |access-date=18 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=14 February 1939}}</ref> without issue.<ref name="EarlSuffolk"/> 
* Lt.-Cdr. Hon. [[Greville Howard (St Ives MP)|Greville Reginald Howard]] (1909–1987), who married Mary Ridehalgh, daughter of William Smith Ridehalgh of Broughton Lodge.<ref name="EarlSuffolk"/>

While she was in India with her sister, her husband died after shrapnel pierced his heart at the [[Battle of Istabulat]] on 21 April 1917 while commanding his battery. He was buried at [[Basra War Cemetery]] in [[Iraq]].<ref name="1917Death">{{cite news |title=EARL OF SUFFOLK KILLED.; Husband of "Daisy" Leiter Meets Death at Front in France. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1917/04/26/98247412.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=26 April 1917}}</ref>  Her eldest son, who became the 20th [[Earl of Suffolk]], died was killed in 1941 by a bomb he was attempting to defuse and was succeeded by his six year old son, [[Michael Howard, 21st Earl of Suffolk]].<ref name="1941Obit">{{cite news |title=Earl of Suffolk and Berkshire Is Killed by Bomb at Age of 35; Peer Whose Grandfather Was Levi Z. Leiter Dies in Blast With His Secretary and Six Soldiers -- Served in France |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1941/05/14/85348860.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=14 May 1941}}</ref>

===Later life===
In 1922, while riding in Charlton Park, she fell from her horse and broke her leg.<ref name="1922Leg">{{cite news |title=COUNTESS OF SUFFOLK HAS FALL, BREAKS LEG; Former Miss Daisy Leiter Injured in accident While Riding a Horse. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1922/08/04/99050450.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=4 August 1922}}</ref> Two years later in 1924, she again fell from her horse and suffered broke several ribs.<ref name="1924Ribs">{{cite news |title=COUNTESS OF SUFFOLK BREAKS RIBS IN FALL; Former Daisy Leiter Has Second Hunting Accident in England in Two Years. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1924/12/10/101625776.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=10 December 1924}}</ref>

Upon her father's death in 1904,<ref name="LZLObit1904">{{cite news |title=LEVI Z. LEITER DIES IN MAINE OF HEART DISEASE; Had Been Ill a Long Time, but Death Was Unexpected. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1904/06/10/120269206.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=10 June 1904}}</ref> he bequeathed her millions of dollars in his will, which stipulated that she had to live four months of every year in the United States.<ref name="1904Will">{{cite news |title=LEVI Z. LEITER'S WILL.; Two-thirds to the Children -- Trust Fund for Lady Curzon. |url=https://timesmachine.nytimes.com/timesmachine/1904/06/21/120269911.pdf |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=21 June 1904}}</ref><ref name="Marriott2012">{{cite book |last1=Marriott |first1=Barbara |title=Legendary Locals of Marana, Oro Valley, and Catalina |date=2012 |publisher=Arcadia Publishing |isbn=9781439650035 |page=56 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Ju_BCQAAQBAJ&pg=PA56 |access-date=24 September 2019 |language=en}}</ref>  In 1930, she agreed to sell the [[Pelican Portrait]] of [[Elizabeth I of England|Queen Elizabeth I]], which was at Charlton House, to [[Spink & Son]] for a rich collector.<ref>[[David Starkey]], Susan Doran, ''Elizabeth: The Exhibition at the National Maritime Museum'' (National Maritime Museum, 2003),  p. 192</ref> In 1934, she bought property on [[Magee [Pima Canyon]]] Road, at the foothills of the [[Santa Catalina Mountains]] in [[Oro Valley, Arizona]] from Matthew Baird III.<ref name="Needham2016">{{cite news |last1=Needham |first1=Sybil |title=In Search of the Countess |url=https://ovhistory.org/in-search-of-the-countess/ |access-date=24 September 2019 |work=Oro Valley Historical Society |date=12 February 2016}}</ref>  In 1935, she hired architect Robert A. Morse to design her a house "that hinted at [[Art Deco|art deco]]" and featured five master bedrooms, air-conditioning, and "a bomb shelter that ran under the perimeter of the house."<ref name="Marriott2008">{{cite book |last1=Marriott |first1=Barbara |last2=Society |first2=Oro Valley Historical |title=Oro Valley |date=2008 |publisher=[[Arcadia Publishing]] |isbn=9780738548340 |page=98 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=loyMh3VlmQ8C&pg=PA98 |access-date=24 September 2019 |language=en}}</ref> She called it "Forest Lodge" and owned the property until 1957 when she felt the nearby city of [[Tucson, Arizona|Tucson]] was encroaching on her property and sold it to the [[Sisters of the Immaculate Heart of Mary]] (which became the [[Immaculate Heart High School (Arizona)|Immaculate Heart High School]]).<ref name="LadySuffolk"/>  She then purchased a 3,500 acre ranch in [[Oracle, Arizona]], which today is part of [[Biosphere 2]].<ref name="Needham2016"/>

In her later years, she became a helicopter pilot and was known to fly from her Cornish home to her suite at [[The Ritz Hotel, London|The Ritz Hotel]] via the [[Battersea Heliport]].<ref name="npg.org">{{cite web |title=Marguerite Hyde ('Daisy', née Leiter), Countess of Suffolk |url=https://www.npg.org.uk/collections/search/person/mp94866/marguerite-hyde-daisy-nee-leiter-countess-of-suffolk |website=www.npg.org.uk |publisher=[[National Portrait Gallery, London]] |access-date=24 September 2019 |language=en}}</ref>  While flying to California to visit her son Cecil, the Dowager Countess of Suffolk and Berkshire had a heart attack and later died at [[Antelope Valley Hospital]] in [[Lancaster, California]] on 5 March 1968.<ref name="CountessofSuffolk1968">{{cite news |title=Margaret Lady Howard Dies; Dowager Countess of Suffolk |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1968/03/06/archives/margaret-lady-howard-dies-dowager-countess-of-suffolk.html?searchResultPosition=1 |access-date=18 September 2019 |work=[[The New York Times]] |date=March 6, 1968}}</ref>

==In popular culture==
During the 2014-2015 exhibition at London's National Portrait Gallery, Margaret was featured among the high-profile American heiresses to marry into British aristocracy.<ref name="MacColl2012">{{cite book |last1=MacColl |first1=Gail |last2=Wallace |first2=Carol McD |title=To Marry an English Lord: Tales of Wealth and Marriage, Sex and Snobbery |date=2012 |publisher=Workman Publishing |isbn=9780761171959 |pages=77, 249, 302, 309, 340 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=UGSE4B506MMC&pg=PA357 |access-date=24 September 2019 |language=en}}</ref> Included in the exhibition were [[Jennie Jerome|Jeanette ('Jennie') Churchill (née Jerome)]], [[Lady Randolph Churchill]], [[Mary Curzon, Baroness Curzon of Kedleston|Mary Victoria (née Leiter), Lady Curzon of Kedleston]], Mary Carolyn Campbell ('May', née Cuyler), [[Sir Philip Grey Egerton, 12th Baronet|Lady Grey-Egerton]], [[Consuelo Montagu, Duchess of Manchester]], [[Consuelo Vanderbilt|Consuelo (née Vanderbilt), Duchess of Marlborough]] (later [[Jacques Balsan|Mrs. Balsan]]), [[John Spencer-Churchill, 10th Duke of Marlborough]], and [[Cornelia, Countess of Craven|Cornelia (née Martin), Countess of Craven]].<ref name="OldTitlesNewMoney">{{cite web |title=Old Titles and New Money: American Heiresses and the British Aristocracy |url=https://www.npg.org.uk/whatson/display/2014/old-titles-and-new-money-american-heiresses-and-the-british-aristocracy.php |website=www.npg.org.uk |publisher=[[National Portrait Gallery, London]] |access-date=17 September 2019}}</ref>
