'''William Lucy''' (November 26, 1933 – September 25, 2024) was an American trade union leader. He served as Secretary-Treasurer of the [[American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees]] (AFSCME) from 1972 to 2010.

==Life==
Lucy was born to Susie and Joseph Lucy in [[Memphis, Tennessee]], on November 26, 1933, and grew up in [[Richmond, California]].<ref name = Roberts>{{cite news|url = https://www.nytimes.com/2024/09/28/us/bill-lucy-dead.html|title = Bill Lucy, 90, Pioneering Labor and Civil Rights Leader|last = Roberts|first = Sam|authorlink = Sam Roberts (journalist)|date = September 30, 2024|accessdate = September 30, 2024|page = A20|newspaper = [[The New York Times]]|url-access = limited}}</ref> In the early 1950s, he served in the U.S. Navy, and studied civil engineering at the [[University of California, Berkeley]] but did not obtain a degree.<ref name = Roberts/> He worked for [[Contra Costa County, California|Contra Costa County]] as a materials and research engineer, where he would work for the next thirteen years. During this period of time, Lucy started working within the labor movement.<ref name=hmwl41>{{cite web |url=http://www.thehistorymakers.org/biography/william-lucy-41 |title=William Lucy |work=History Makers |accessdate=March 16, 2018}}</ref>

Lucy served on the board of directors of the [[NAACP]].<ref name=naacp>{{cite web |url=http://www.naacp.org/naacp-board-of-directors/william-lucy/ |title=William Lucy |publisher=[[NAACP]] |accessdate=March 21, 2024| archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210308221803/http://www.naacp.org/naacp-board-of-directors/william-lucy/| archive-date=March 8, 2021}}</ref>

Lucy married Dorothea Raider in 1953; they had three children and were married until her death in 2000.<ref name = Roberts/> Lucy died at his home in Washington, D.C., on September 25, 2024, at the age of 90.<ref>{{cite web|title=Revered Labor Leader William Lucy Dies at 90|url=https://www.washingtoninformer.com/bill-lucy-afscme-labor-leader/|publisher=[[The Washington Informer]]|date=September 25, 2024|access-date=September 26, 2024}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |date=September 25, 2024 |title=Labor Movement Celebrates Extraordinary Life and Career of Bill Lucy {{!}} AFL-CIO |url=https://aflcio.org/press/releases/labor-movement-celebrates-extraordinary-life-and-career-bill-lucy |access-date=September 26, 2024 |website=aflcio.org |language=en}}</ref>

[[File:I Am a Man - Diorama of Memphis Sanitation Workers Strike - National Civil Rights Museum - Downtown Memphis - Tennessee - USA.jpg|thumb|upright|left|Diorama of Memphis Strikers featuring the famous slogan "I <u>Am</u> A Man!"]]

==Labor movement==
Lucy became a member of the [[American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees]] (AFSCME) Local #1675 in 1956 and was elected as its president in 1965. He began working full-time at AFSCME's national headquarters in Washington, D.C. the following year as associate director of legislation and community affairs.<ref name=hmwl41 />

Lucy served as Secretary-Treasurer of AFSCME from 1972 until retiring from the position in 2010. During that period, Lucy co-founded the [[Coalition of Black Trade Unionists]] (CBTU), along with fellow black unionists [[Nelson Edwards]], William Simons, [[Charles Hayes (politician)|Charles Hayes]], and [[Cleveland Robinson]].<ref name=bpaah>{{cite web| url=http://www.blackpast.org/aah/lucy-william-bill-1933 |title=William Lucy |work=Black Past |date=December 19, 2009 |accessdate=March 22, 2018}}</ref> Lucy was elected its first president and continued to serve in that capacity until 2013, when the role was assumed by [[Terrence Melvin]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.cbtu.org/president.php|title=CBTU – Coalition of Black Trade Unionists|website=www.cbtu.org|access-date=May 15, 2016}}</ref>

Lucy was elected president of [[Public Services International]] in 1994, the first African American to hold the post.<ref name=hmwl41 /> In 1995, he was appointed to the [[AFL–CIO]] executive council.<ref>{{cite news |last=Staff |title=William Lucy co-founded and pioneered the Coalition of Black Trade Unionists |newspaper=[[St. Louis American]] |date=September 7, 2022 |url=http://www.stlamerican.com/black_history/william-lucy-co-founded-and-pioneered-the-coalition-of-black-trade-unionists/article_a757e5d2-2b97-11ed-b1f1-07b33d111fc5.html |accessdate=September 25, 2022}}</ref> He has also served as vice-president for the AFL–CIO's Maritime Trades, Professional Employees, and Industrial Union departments.<ref name=naacp />

==Memphis sanitation strike==
{{Main|Memphis sanitation strike}}
In 1968, as part of his leadership role with AFSCME, Lucy lent his support to [[Martin Luther King Jr.]] and the mostly black sanitation and other service workers in Memphis who were striking for better wages and benefits. In spite of [[assassination of Martin Luther King Jr.|King's assassination]] in April 1968, Lucy continued the work in Memphis, helping see the strike to a successful resolution.<ref name=bpaah /> Lucy is credited with the famous slogan, "I <u>Am</u> A Man!" that became the rallying call for the Memphis strikers.<ref name=hmwl41 />

==Free South Africa Movement==
{{Main|Free South Africa Movement}}
Lucy co-founded the [[Free South Africa Movement]], a grassroots [[Internal resistance to apartheid|anti-apartheid]] campaign, and was part of the movement for over 20 years. He was part of an AFL–CIO delegation monitoring elections when [[Nelson Mandela]] was elected the first black president of the Republic of South Africa.<ref name=naacp />
