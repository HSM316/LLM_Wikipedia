'''Beverley Palesa Ditsie''' (born 1971) is a South African [[lesbian]] activist, artist, and [[Filmmaking|filmmaker]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|title = African Sexualities: A Reader|last = Matebeni|first = Zethu|publisher = Pamabazuka Press|year = 2011|isbn = 978-0857490162|pages = 50–52|editor-last = Tamale|editor-first = Sylvia|chapter-url = https://books.google.com/books?id=xSqIrrswbG0C&q=%22Bev+Palesa+Ditsie%22&pg=PP1|chapter = TRACKS: Researching Sexualities Walking AbOUT the City of Johannesburg}}</ref><ref>{{Citation|title=A discussion on LGBTIQA+ rights in South Africa|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=s0ibovapde8|language=en|access-date=2019-12-17}}</ref> Ditsie is one of the founders of the gay rights organization [[Gay and Lesbian Organization of Witwatersrand]].

In speaking about the importance of considering [[LGBT]] rights in the context of [[human rights]] at the [[World Conference on Women, 1995|4th UN World Conference on Women in Beijing in 1995]], she became the first openly [[lesbian]] woman to do. It was also the first time that the [[United Nations]] was addressed about LGBT issues,<ref name=":2" /> with Ditsie saying, “if the world conference on women is to address the concerns of all women, it must similarly recognise that discrimination based on sexual orientation is a violation of basic human rights”.

== Biography ==
Ditsie was born in Orlando West, [[Soweto]], South Africa in 1971.

== Activism ==
Ditsie is one of the organizers of the first [[Pride parade|Pride March]] in [[South Africa]].<ref name=":1">{{Cite news|url = http://www.polarimagazine.com/interviews/beverley-i-interview-with-beverley-ditsie/|title = Beverly & I: Interview with Beverley Ditsie|last = Langan|first = Michael|date = 2014|work = Polari Magazine|access-date = 24 February 2016}}</ref> The parade took place in Johannesburg in 1990.<ref name=":0" /> Ditsie and her friend, [[Simon Nkoli]], another activist, worked together on creating the pride event after Simon had the idea from his visit to [[United States|America]].<ref>{{Cite book|chapter-url=https://books.google.com/books?id=FdNz05DF6JcC&pg=PA2|title=Pride: Protest and Celebration|last=Mohlamme|first=Charity|publisher=Fanele|year=2006|isbn=978-1-77009-261-7|editor-last=de Waal|editor-first=Shaun|pages=36|chapter=It Was Part Of Our Coming Out...|editor-last2=Manion|editor-first2=Anthony}}</ref> During the event, she spoke on live TV, becoming a "cultural icon" and also a target for people's hatred and bigotry.<ref name=":0" /> She states that she had to be "escorted for about two weeks after the pride march" for her safety.<ref name=":0" /> Ditsie is critical of current pride activities because she sees a cultural and racial divide between LGBT people in South Africa.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.seattletimes.com/nation-world/gay-rights-in-name-only-in-the-lsquorainbow-nationrsquo/|title=Gay Rights in Name Only in the 'Rainbow Nation'|last=Beason|first=Tyrone|date=16 August 2014|work=The Seattle Times|access-date=24 February 2016}}</ref>

Ditsie spoke at the [[United Nations]] Conference on Women in 1995, and was the person to address gay and lesbian rights before a U.N. 4th World Conference on Women."<ref>{{Cite book|title = Namibia's Rainbow Project: Gay Rights in an African Nation|last = Lorway|first = Robert|publisher = Indiana University Press|year = 2014|isbn = 9780253015273|pages = 19|url = https://books.google.com/books?id=O9wMBQAAQBAJ&q=%22Bev+Palesa+Ditsie%22&pg=PA19}}</ref> She attempted to convince U.N. delegates to "adopt resolutions recognizing [[sexual diversity]]."<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://newspaperarchive.com/ca/alberta/medicine-hat/medicine-hat-news/1995/09-14/page-12?tag=beverley+ditsie&rtserp=tags/?pep=beverley-ditsie|title=UN Delegates Split on Lesbianism|last=Manthorpe|first=Jonathan|date=14 September 1995|work=Medicine Hat News|url-access=subscription|access-date=24 February 2016|via=Newspaper Archive}}</ref>
