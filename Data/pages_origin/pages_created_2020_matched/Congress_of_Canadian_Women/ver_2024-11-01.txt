{{Short description|Canadian feminist organization}}
The '''Congress of Canadian Women''' (French: '''Congrès des femmes canadiennes''') was a [[Feminism|feminist]] organization created in March 1950 by the merger of several organizations, including the Housewives and Consumers Association. It was affiliated with the [[Women's International Democratic Federation]].<ref name=":1" /> The Congress lobbied for women's equality including measures such as [[Equal pay for equal work|equal pay]] and public [[Child care|daycare]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://famouscanadianwomen.com/timeline/timeline1950-1959.htm|title=Famous Canadian Women's Historic Timeline - 1950-1959|website=famouscanadianwomen.com}}</ref> [[Rae Luckock]] was the organization's founding president.<ref name=":1">{{Cite news |date=1949-11-05 |title=Conference for Canadian Women |pages=8 |work=Star-Phoenix |url=https://www.newspapers.com/article/star-phoenix-conference-for-canadian-wom/134841882/ |access-date=2023-11-08 |via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> Former [[Labor-Progressive Party]] [[House of Commons of Canada|Member of Parliament]] [[Dorise Nielsen]] was also involved in founding the organization.<ref name=":0">{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=e_hFKQeC3JgC&q=%22congress+of+Canadian+women%22+nielsen&pg=PA215|title=A Great Restlessness: The Life and Politics of Dorise Nielsen|first=Faith|last=Johnston|date=11 February 2018|publisher=Univ. of Manitoba Press|isbn=9780887553066|via=Google Books}}</ref> The CCW was largely led by women associated with the communist [[Labor-Progressive Party]]<ref>{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=0Fk9DgAAQBAJ&q=%22congress+of+Canadian+women%22+&pg=PA206|title=Progressive Heritage: The Evolution of a Politically Radical Literary Tradition in Canada|first=James|last=Doyle|date=1 January 2006|publisher=Wilfrid Laurier Univ. Press|isbn=9780889208292|via=Google Books}}</ref> and was also involved with the [[peace movement]] during the [[Cold War]],<ref>{{Cite news |date=1963-10-05 |title=Women to March on Ottawa |pages=58 |work=The Montreal Star |url=https://www.newspapers.com/article/the-montreal-star-women-to-march-on-otta/134861323/ |access-date=2023-11-08 |via=Newspapers.com}}</ref> facilitating meetings between people from the Soviet Union and Canadians, by inviting them to visit Canada.<ref name="CCW President">{{cite news|title=May visitors from Russia|url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=viBUAAAAIBAJ&dq=rae%20luckock&pg=5219%2C2936835|accessdate=2012-03-03|newspaper=The Leader-Post|date=1956-01-23|author=Canadian Press|location=Regina, Saskatchewan|page=6}}</ref>

There is a file of papers related to the Congress of Canadian Women in the Canadian Women's Movement Archive at the [[University of Ottawa]] Library.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Congress of Canadian Women / Congrès des femmes canadiennes |url=https://biblio.uottawa.ca/atom/index.php/congress-of-canadian-women-congres-des-femmes-canadiennes |access-date=2023-11-08 |website=University of Ottawa Archives and Special Collections}}</ref> The [[Diefenbunker|Diefenbunker Museum]] in Ontario holds a peace campaign pin produced in the 1950s by the Congress of Canadian Women.<ref>{{Cite web |last=Hobson |first=Emily |date=2023-03-10 |title=25 Artifacts: I am a Woman for Peace |url=https://diefenbunker.ca/i-am-a-woman-for-peace/ |access-date=2023-11-08 |website=Diefenbunker Museum |language=en-CA}}</ref>
