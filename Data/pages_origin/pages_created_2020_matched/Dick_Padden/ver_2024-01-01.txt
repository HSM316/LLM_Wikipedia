'''Richard Joseph Padden''' (September 17, 1870 – October 31, 1922), nicknamed "'''Brains'''", was an American professional baseball player, born in [[Wheeling, West Virginia]], who played mainly as a [[second baseman]] in [[Major League Baseball]] for nine seasons from {{mlby|1896}} to {{mlby|1905}}.<ref name="reference">{{cite web|url=https://www.baseball-reference.com/players/p/paddedi01.shtml|title=Dick Padden|work=baseball-reference.com|access-date=31 December 2009}}</ref>

After playing a season and a half in the [[minor league baseball|minor leagues]], the right-handed [[infielder]] began his major league career for the [[Pittsburgh Pirates]]. He played three seasons in Pittsburgh, from 1896 to {{mlby|1898}}, before playing one season for the [[Washington Senators (1891–1899)|Washington Senators]] in {{mlby|1899}}. He returned to the minor leagues for the 1900 season, where he was the [[player-manager]] for the [[Chicago White Sox]], then a minor league team. When the Chicago club entered the [[American League]], a major league, the following season, he moved on to play one season for the [[St. Louis Cardinals]], before becoming [[Captain (sports)|Captain]] of the [[St. Louis Browns]] from {{mlby|1902}} and 1905. In total, Padden played in 874 games, and collected 814 [[hit (baseball)|hits]] in 3545 [[at bat]]s, for a lifetime [[batting average (baseball)|batting average]] of .258. He finished in the league's top-ten finishers in being [[hit by pitch]]es six times, including a league-leading 18 in 1904.

Padden's post-career activities included duties as a talent [[scout (sports)|scout]] for the [[St. Louis Browns]] and the [[Washington Senators (1901–60)|Washington Senators]], as well a lengthy career in the [[flint glass]] industry in [[Ohio]]. After retiring, he attempted to gain the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]] nomination for the 1912 mayoral race in his hometown of [[Martin's Ferry, Ohio]]. He died there, in 1922, at the age of 52 of [[apoplexy]].

==Career==

===Pittsburgh===
Padden began his professional career with the Roanoke Magicians, a minor league club in the Virginia State League in 1895, where he played with and [[manager (baseball)|managed]] the team to a 52–74 [[winning percentage|win–loss record]].<ref name="paddenminor">{{cite web|url=https://www.baseball-reference.com/minors/player.cgi?id=padden001ric|title=Dick Padden (minor league)|work=baseball-reference.com|access-date=9 December 2009}}</ref>  During the 1896 season, the [[Pittsburgh Pirates]] [[manager (baseball)|manager]], [[Connie Mack]], was seeking a replacement for [[Harry Truby]], his second baseman, who was not performing well, when Padden caught his attention.<ref name="macht126">Macht, p. 126</ref>  Padden was playing for the Toronto Canadiens in the [[International League|Eastern League]], and had a reputation of being a smart and quick player with good instincts, as well as a "careful, timely batsman".<ref name="paddenminor"/><ref name="macht126"/><ref name="spink286">Spink, p. 286</ref>  Based on his need to upgrade his second base position, and Padden's good reputation, Mack traded Truby for him.<ref name="macht126"/>  He began his major league career shortly thereafter on July 15, one day after Truby's final game in the majors.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.baseball-reference.com/teams/PIT/1896.shtml|title=1896 Pittsburgh Pirates Batting, Pitching, & Fielding Statistics|work=baseball-reference.com|access-date=9 December 2009}}</ref>  When Padden did not bat well to start the season, Mack and Pittsburgh's president and part owner, [[William Kerr (baseball)|William Kerr]], began to feud over the decision, with Kerr questioning Mack over whether they should have traded Truby.<ref name="macht126"/>  However, his hitting improved and he completed the season with a .242 [[batting average (baseball)|batting average]] in 61&nbsp;[[games played]].<ref name="macht126"/><ref name="brprofile">{{cite web|url=https://www.baseball-reference.com/players/p/paddedi01.shtml#trans|title=Player Profile: Dick Padden |work=baseball-reference.com|access-date=9 December 2009}}</ref>

In {{mlby|1897}}, with the Pirates, he led all National League second basemen with 134&nbsp;games played, and 369&nbsp;[[putout]]s.<ref name="retrosheet"/><ref name="1897piratesroster">{{cite web|url=http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/1897/UPPIT01897.htm|title=The 1897 Pittsburgh Pirates Regular Season Roster|work=retrosheet.org|publisher=Retrosheet, Inc|access-date=10 December 2009}}</ref>  Besides games played, he established career highs in several batting categories that season, including; 517&nbsp;[[at bat]]s, 84&nbsp;[[run (baseball)|runs]] scored, 146&nbsp;[[hit (baseball)|hits]], 10&nbsp;[[triple (baseball)|triples]], and tied his career high in [[home run]]s with two.<ref name="retrosheet">{{cite web|url=http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/P/Ppaddd102.htm|title=Dick Padden|work=retrosheet.org|publisher=Retrosheet, Inc|access-date=9 December 2009}}</ref>  He made 128&nbsp;appearances in {{mlby|1898}}, again as their starting second baseman. His statistical output dropped from his previous season; his batting average lowered to .257, and scored 61&nbsp;runs in 463&nbsp;at bats.<ref name="1898piratesroster">{{cite web|url=http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/1898/UPPIT01898.htm|title=The 1898 Pittsburgh Pirates Regular Season Roster|work=retrosheet.org|publisher=Retrosheet, Inc|access-date=10 December 2009}}</ref>

===Washington===
On December&nbsp;14, 1898, after the season was completed, Padden was [[Trade (sports)|traded]], along with [[Jack O'Brien (outfielder)|Jack O'Brien]] and [[Jimmy Slagle]], to the [[Washington Senators (1891–1899)|Washington Senators]] in exchange for [[Heinie Reitz]].<ref name="brprofile"/>  He played the 1899&nbsp;season in Washington, and appeared in 134&nbsp;games as their starting [[shortstop]].<ref name="1899nationalsroster">{{cite web|url=http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/1899/UPWSN01899.htm|title=The 1899 Washington Nationals Regular Season Roster |work=retrosheet.org|publisher=Retrosheet, Inc|access-date=10 December 2009}}</ref>  He had a batting average of .277, established his career high in [[stolen base]]s&nbsp;(27), and was [[Ejection (sports)|ejected]] from the game by the [[umpire (baseball)|umpire]] three times, which led the league.<ref name="retrosheet"/>

===Chicago===
Following the 1899 season, the Senators franchise ceased operations, which resulted in Padden's sale, along with O'Brien, to the [[Detroit Tigers]] of the [[American League]], which was a minor league at the time.<ref name="retrosheet"/><ref name="nationalsfranchise">{{cite web|url=http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/W/FR_WSN.htm|title=Washington Nationals (1891–1899)|work=retrosheet.org|publisher=Retrosheet, Inc|access-date=10 December 2009}}</ref>  However, before the {{mlby|1900}} season began, he joined the [[Chicago White Sox]], also a minor league team, as their [[player-manager]] instead.<ref name="spink286"/><ref name="retrosheet"/> On May 16, he was involved in a physical altercation with [[Ducky Holmes]] and another [[Detroit]] player.<ref name="bd">{{cite news|last=Bryson|first=Bill|title=When Teams Were Full of Fight|work=Baseball Digest|volume=10, No. 3.|pages=41–42}}</ref> While playing a [[doubleheader (baseball)|doubleheader]] on September&nbsp;16, against Connie Mack and his Milwaukee team, Padden suggested to Mack that the second game be shortened to five innings due to the fact that the first game lasted just over three hours, and they might not finish the second game before darkness.<ref name="macht126"/>  Mack, who estimated that he could get five more good innings from his game one [[starting pitcher]], [[Rube Waddell]], quickly agreed.<ref name="macht126"/>  Padden's suggestion backfired, and turned into a successful strategy for Mack, whose team, with Waddell pitching, won the game.<ref name="macht126"/> Despite that incident, Padden and his leadership skills led the White Sox to the American League championship that season.<ref name="james91">James, p. 91</ref>

===St. Louis===
The White Sox stayed with the American League as they became a major league in {{mlby|1901}}, but Padden moved on to play for the [[St. Louis Cardinals]] of the National League.<ref name="brprofile"/>  He was the team's starting second baseman, and played in a total of 123&nbsp;games.<ref name="1901stlouisroster">{{cite web|url=http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/1901/UPSLN01901.htm|title=The 1901 St. Louis Cardinals Regular Season Roster|work=retrosheet.org|publisher=Retrosheet, Inc|access-date=10 December 2009}}</ref>  He had a batting average of .256 that season, and again led the league in ejections, with five; however, he stole 26&nbsp;bases, he had 62&nbsp;[[run batted in|RBIs]], his highest single season total.<ref name="retrosheet"/>

After his lone season with the Cardinals, Padden, along with many of his teammates deserted to the newly created [[St. Louis Browns]] franchise, which was scheduled to begin playing in 1902 in the American League.<ref name="timeswar">{{cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1913/12/07/archives/war-that-crippled-national-league-ban-johnsons-campaign-swept-star.html|title=WAR THAT CRIPPLED NATIONAL LEAGUE|date=December 7, 1913|work=[[The New York Times]]|access-date=9 December 2009}}</ref>  Padden was Captain of the Browns during his time on the team.<ref name=obit>{{cite news|url=http://www.thedeadballera.com/Obits/Obits_P/Padden.Dick.Obit.html|title=Padden Dies of Apoplexy|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=November 1, 1922|access-date=2009-12-09}}</ref>  In both 1902 and {{mlby|1904}}, he was the Browns' starting second baseman, and were the only two of his four seasons with the club in which he played in more than 29&nbsp;games.<ref name="1902brownsroster">{{cite web|url=http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/1902/UPSLA01902.htm|title=The 1902 St. Louis Browns Regular Season Roster|work=retrosheet.org|publisher=Retrosheet, Inc|access-date=10 December 2009}}</ref><ref name="1904brownsroster">{{cite web|url=http://www.retrosheet.org/boxesetc/1904/UPSLA01904.htm|title=The 1904 St. Louis Browns Regular Season Roster|work=retrosheet.org|publisher=Retrosheet, Inc|access-date=10 December 2009}}</ref>  He led the league in [[hit by pitch|hit by a pitch]]es with 18 in {{mlby|1904}}, the only time in which he led the league in this category, although he did finish in the league's top-ten five times previously.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.baseball-reference.com/leagues/AL/1904-batting-leaders.shtml|title=1904 American League Batting Leaders|work=Baseball-Reference.com|access-date=9 December 2009}}</ref>  On May 19, 1905, after 16&nbsp;games, Padden was released by the Browns, and his major league career came to an end.<ref name="retrosheet"/>

==Post-major league career==
Padden was the player-manager for the minor league team [[St. Paul Saints]] of the [[American Association (1902–1997)|American Association]] for the 1906 season.<ref name="paddenminor"/> He had a batting average of .288 that year, and hit three home runs.<ref name="paddenminor"/> He stayed with the Saints for the 1907 season, but as a player only.<ref name="paddenminor"/>  After 52 games played, his season ended when he sprained his ankle. After his injury, he and his Saints manager, Mr. Ashenbach attempted to buy the [[Daytona Beach]] team of the [[International League|Eastern League]], but were unsuccessful.<ref name="paddenherald">{{cite news|url=http://chroniclingamerica.loc.gov/lccn/sn83045433/1907-08-26/ed-1/seq-6/;words=ty+Cobb+Ty|title=Baseball Notes and Comment|date=August 26, 1907|work=chroniclingamerica.loc.gov|page=6|publisher=[[The Washington Herald]]|access-date=9 December 2009}}</ref>  He had hoped that his ankle would heal, so that he could be ready for the next season, but he never appeared as a player after the injury.<ref name="paddenminor"/><ref name="paddenherald"/>

Following his retirement as a player, Padden returned to his old team and became a talent scout for the Browns in 1909.<ref>{{cite news|title=Browns Sign Dick Padden|work=[[Brownsville Daily Herald]]|date=July 7, 1909|page=4}}</ref> He also became a talent scout for the Senators and worked in the [[flint glass]] industry in [[Ohio]].<ref name="flintglass">{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ZFcMAQAAIAAJ&q=%22Dick+Padden%22&pg=RA1-PA13|title="Dick" Padden Is Dead|year=1922|work=The American flint, Volume 14|publisher=American Flint Glass Workers' Union|pages=13–14|access-date=9 December 2009}}</ref> [[Alfred Henry Spink]] described him as "one of the real foxy fellows of the baseball world. He could field beautifully and was never better than when being hard driven."<ref name="spink286"/> Padden retired to [[Martins Ferry, Ohio]] where in 1912, he tried to secure the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]] nomination for the mayoral race.<ref>{{cite news|title=Base Ball Notes|work=[[Waterloo Reporter]]|date=May 17, 1911|page=12}}</ref> He died in Martins Ferry of [[apoplexy]] on October&nbsp;31, 1922 at the age of 52, and is interred at St. Marys Cemetery.<ref name=retrosheet/><ref name=obit/>
