'''Jean-Louis Bourgeois''' (July 4, 1940 – December 8, 2022) was an American author and the son of artist [[Louise Bourgeois]] and art historian [[Robert Goldwater]]. Bourgeois studied literature and architectural history at [[Harvard University]].

In 1969 and 1970 Bourgeois worked at [[Artforum]] before becoming interested in the production and history of mud brick architecture.<ref name=walrus2004>Alex Ulam.  [http://www.goodwork7.org/walrus.pdf "The Clown. How to tell jokes that win friends and influence people in an ancient city in sub-Saharan Africa] The Walrus. July/August 2004. pp.24–5 Bourgeois intends to translate the article into Funny.</ref> He was the author of the volume ''Spectacular Vernacular: the Adobe Tradition'' (with photographs taken by Carollee Pelos)<ref>Jean-Louis Bourgeois, Carollee Pelos. Spectacular Vernacular: a New Appreciation of Traditional Desert Architecture. Peregrine Smith Books, 1983. {{ISBN|9780879051440}}</ref> which established him as an expert on the subject.<ref>[https://www.nytimes.com/2006/10/08/realestate/08deal1.html?_r=1 7 Stories of Water in a 2-Story Building] by Josh Barbanel, The New York Times. Published: October 8, 2006.</ref> He owned a home in [[Djenne]], [[Mali]] and was actively involved in architectural conservation efforts there including the preservation of the world's largest adobe building the [[Great Mosque of Djenne]],<ref>Deidre d'Entremont. [http://www.culturalsurvival.org/publications/cultural-survival-quarterly/mali/djenne-project-mali-jean-louis-bourgeois-coordinator The Djenne Project, Mali: Jean Louis Bourgeois, Coordinator.] ''Cultural Survival Quarterly'' 25.2 (Summer 2001) Endangered Languages, Endangered Lives.</ref> and wrote on the subject.<ref>Jean-Louis Bourgeois. [https://www.jstor.org/pss/3336477?cookieSet=1 The History of the Great Mosques of Djenné]. African Arts Vol. 20, No. 3 (May, 1987), pp. 54-63+90-92</ref> While living in Djennê, Bourgeois opposed the Talo Dam project, and became a fixture in the city's cultural life.<ref name=walrus2004 /> He appeared in the 2008 documentary film on his mother ''[[Louise Bourgeois: The Spider, the Mistress, and the Tangerine]]''.<ref>[https://www.imdb.com/title/tt1386654/ In ''Louise Bourgeois: The Spider, the Mistress, and the Tangerine'']</ref>  Bourgeois owned an adobe house in [[Taos, New Mexico]], and also wrote on the [[Southwestern United States|Southwestern]] [[Native Americans in the United States|Native American]] Adobe tradition.<ref>[[Ianto Evans]], Michael G. Smith, Linda Smiley. ''The Hand-Sculpted House: A Philosophical and Practical Guide to Building a Cob Cottage''. Chelsea Green Publishing, 2002 {{ISBN|9781890132347}}  p.265</ref><ref>Bourgeois, Jean-Louis. Vernacular Architecture in the Desert. in Joseph F. Kennedy, Michael Smith, Catherine Wanek (eds) ''The Art of Natural Building: Design, Construction, Resources''. 6th Edition. New Society Publishers, 2001 {{ISBN|9780865714335}}</ref>

[[File:393-393 West Street 6 Weehawken Street from north.jpg|thumb|392–393 West Street 6 Weehawken Street from north]]

In December 2016 Bourgeois announced he was giving his $4 million historic house in New York City to the [[Ramapough Mountain Indians|Ramapough]] [[Lenape]] Native American nation; they intend to use it as a meeting house.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Smith-Slogan|first1=Christy|title=Millionaire returning $4M piece of Manhattan to Indian Tribe|url=https://nypost.com/2016/12/18/millionaire-returning-4m-trophy-house-to-indian-tribe/|accessdate=22 December 2016|publisher=New York Post|date=December 18, 2016}}</ref> The building, located at 6 Weehawken Street in the West Village (also known as 392–393 West Street) was formerly a historic public market. However, he reneged on his plan.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://nypost.com/2019/11/02/millionaire-reneges-on-promise-to-give-west-village-property-back-to-american-indian-tribe/|title = Millionaire reneges on promise to give West Village property back to American Indian tribe|date = 2 November 2019}}</ref>

== Death ==
Bourgeois died at his home in New York City on December 8, 2022, at the age of 82.<ref name="obit">{{Cite web|url=https://www.legacy.com/us/obituaries/nytimes/name/jean-louis-bourgeois-obituary?id=38347597/|title = JEAN-LOUIS BOURGEOIS 1940–2022| website=[[Legacy.com]] |date = 9 December 2022}}</ref>
