'''Ryan Andr√© Brasseaux''' (born 1976) is an American [[scholar]] of vernacular American [[music]]. He has served as the Dean of [[Davenport College]] at [[Yale University]] since 2011.<ref>http://www.yaledailynews.com/news/2011/aug/05/ryan-andre-brasseaux-grd-08-picked-davenport-dean/</ref>

He is a leading expert on the history of Cajun music and a frequent collaborator with the Grammy-nominated Lost Bayou Ramblers.

==Education==
Brasseaux did his undergraduate work in anthropology at the [[University of Louisiana at Lafayette]] and earned an M.A. from [[Louisiana State University]]. He received a M.A. and M. Phil. from Yale in 2008 and his Ph.D. in 2011.<ref name="DC Dean Profile">{{cite web|title=Dean Ryan Brasseaux|url=http://davenport.yalecollege.yale.edu/people/dean-ryan-brasseaux|publisher=Yale University|accessdate=June 2, 2014}}</ref> He was awarded a Beinecke Research Fellowship, the Lamar Research Scholar, Kinney/Tesoro Fellowships from the Howard R. Lamar Center for the Study of Frontiers and Borders, and a Prize Teaching Fellowship for excellence in undergraduate instruction and mentorship.<ref>http://www.yale.edu/amstud/gradstudents.html; http://etd.lsu.edu/docs/available/etd-11102004-152234/</ref>

In 2007, Brasseaux helped found Public Humanities at Yale, a graduate student initiative that evolved into an ongoing lecture series and a Masters of Arts program.<ref>{{Cite web | url=http://www.yale.edu/amstud/publichumanities/ |title = Welcome &#124; American Studies}}</ref>

==Work==
He is the author of ''Cajun Breakdown: The Emergence of an American-Made Music'' (Oxford University Press, 2009), co-editor of ''Accordions, Fiddles, Two Step & Swing: A Cajun Music Reader'' (2006), and co-author of ''Stir the Pot: The History of Cajun Cuisine'' (2005).<ref>http://www.louisianabookfestival.org/lbf2006/Authors/Author_Bio_Page.htm; http://cls.louisiana.edu/new-releases/accordions-fiddles.shtml</ref><ref>http://www.oup.com/us/catalog/general/subject/Music/MusicHistoryAmerican/?view=usa&ci=9780195343069</ref>

Brasseaux has presented his work across the [[United States]], including invited lectures in [[New York City]], [[Yale University]], to the Fulbright Institute, and as the keynote speaker at [[Nashville, Tennessee|Nashville]]'s International Country Music Conference.<ref>[http://www.internationalcountrymusic.org/schedule.html International Country Music Conference - Schedule<!-- Bot generated title -->]</ref> He has also lectured extensively, accompanied by [[Grammy]]-nominated Cajun band the [[Lost Bayou Ramblers]], on the origins and evolution of Cajun music.<ref>[http://www.lsue.edu/acadgate/music/lostbayouramblers.htm The Lost Bayou Ramblers<!-- Bot generated title -->] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140201181748/http://www.lsue.edu/acadgate/music/lostbayouramblers.htm |date=2014-02-01 }}</ref> In the wake of [[Hurricane Katrina]], Brasseaux worked as research associate for the nationally syndicated public radio program ''[[American Routes]]'' hosted by Nick Spitzer.<ref>Samuel G. Freedman (September 7, 2005). This Song Goes Out to You, Big Easy. The New York Times;</ref>

==Personal life==
He is the son of Cajun historian [[Carl A. Brasseaux]]. Brasseaux is married and has two children.<ref name="DC Dean Profile"/>
