'''Mauro Martino''' is an Italian artist, designer and researcher.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.mamartino.com/information.html|title=Mauro Martino. About|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> He is the founder and director of the Visual Artificial Intelligence Lab at IBM Research, and Professor of Practice at [[Northeastern University]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://camd.northeastern.edu/faculty/mauro-martino/|title=Northeastern University, CAMD Art&Design|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref> He graduated from [[Polytechnic University of Milan]], and was a research affiliate with the [[MIT Senseable City Lab|Senseable City Lab]] at [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology|MIT]].

His works have been published in "The Best American Infographics" in 2015<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|title=The Best American Infographics 2015|last=Cook|first=Gareth|publisher=Houghton Mifflin Harcourt|year=2015|isbn=978-0544542709|location=|pages=150–151}}</ref> and 2016 editions<ref name=":1">{{Cite book|title=The Best American Infographics 2016|last=Cook|first=Gareth|publisher=Houghton Mifflin Harcourt|year=2016|isbn=978-0544556386|location=|pages=36–37}}</ref> and have been shown at international festivals and exhibitions including [[Ars Electronica]],<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://ars.electronica.art/origin/2011/07/27/sensing-place-placing-sense-symposium/|title=Ars Electronica 2011. Sensing Place / Placing Sense – Symposium|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> [[RIXC]] Art Science Festival,<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://rixc.org/en/exhibitions/2015/open-fields-rixc-art-science-festival-2016-exhibition/|title=OPEN FIELDS. RIXC Art Science Festival 2016 Exhibition|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> Global Exchange at [[Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts|Lincoln Center]],<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://lincolncenterglobalexchange.org/conference/2015|title=Lincoln Center Global Exchange 2015|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> TEDx Cambridge THRIVE and TEDx Riga<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://2016.tedxriga.com/schedule/mauro-martino/|title=TEDxRiga. Mauro Martino|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> as well as the [[Serpentine Gallery]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.edge.org/event/edge-serpentine-gallery-maps-for-the-21st-century|title=Edge-Serpentine Gallery-MAPS FOR THE 21ST CENTURY|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> His work is in the permanent collection at [[Ars Electronica Center]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://ars.electronica.art/center/en/formafluens/|title=Ars Electronica. Understanding AI|access-date=June 20, 2019}}</ref> In 2017, the project ''Network Earth'' received the [[National Science Foundation]]'s award as Best Scientific Video.<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=https://www.nsf.gov/news/special_reports/scivis/vizzies_winners_2017.jsp|title=Vizzies Visualization Challenge|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref> The project ''AI Portraits'' won the 2019 [[Webby Award|Webby]] People's Voice Award in the category NetArt''.''<ref name=":3">{{Cite web|url=https://www.webbyawards.com/winners/2019/websites/general-websites/netart/ai-portraits/|title=Webby People's Voice Award 2019 - NetArt|access-date=June 20, 2019}}</ref>

Mauro Martino is a pioneer in the use of the [[artificial neural network]] in sculpture.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.aiartonline.com/art/mauro-martino-nima-dehmami-luca-stornaiuolo/|title=NeurIPS Workshop on Machine Learning for Creativity and Design|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://fuorisalonemagazine.it/en/people/article/1156/mauro-martino|title=Fuorisalone.it Magazine. People|access-date=31 March 2019}}</ref>

== Notable works ==

*''AI Portraits'' <ref>{{Cite web|url=https://aiportraits.com|title=AI Portraits|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> is a research project that uses [[artificial neural network]] to reconstruct a portrait of a person. The AI system was trained on a dataset that included millions of photos of actors and actresses.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://mashable.com/video/ai-portraits-celebrity-doppelganger/#PxK05Uie8kq0|title=Mashable.com. AI Portraits uses code to turn you into a celebrity|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> 
* ''WonderNet'' <ref>{{Cite web|url=http://netwonder.net|title=WonderNet|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> was developed in collaboration with [[Albert-László Barabási]] at the Center for Complex Network Research<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.northeastern.edu/research/centers/center-for-complex-network-research-ccnr/|title=Center for Complex Network Research (CCNR)|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref> at Northeastern University. WonderNet includes 8 data sculptures which represent 8 different "data-stories" (e.g., art network, flavor network, fake news network, etc.). It was presented at the IEEE VIS 2018 Arts Program in Berlin.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://vimeo.com/305763966|title=IEEE VIS 2018 Arts Program|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref>
* ''Forma Fluens'' <ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.formafluens.io/index.html#second|title=Forma Fluens|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> uses the world’s largest doodle data set by [[Google]] [[Quick, Draw!|Quick Draw]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://flowingdata.com/2017/06/16/looking-for-culture-expression-in-50-million-doodles/|title=Flowing Data. Looking for cultural expression in 50 million doodles|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref> This project was presented at ''123 DATA'' design exhibition in Paris.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://123data.paris|title=123 DATA|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref>
* ''Charting Culture'' maps cultural mobility, tracking the births and deaths of notable individuals, from 600 BC to the present day.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://flowingdata.com/2014/08/04/cultural-history-via-where-notable-people-died/|title=Flowing Data. Cultural history via where notable people died|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.nature.com/news/humanity-s-cultural-history-captured-in-5-minute-film-1.15650|title=Nature.com. Humanity's cultural history captured in 5-minute film|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref> ''Charting Culture'' is one of the most viewed videos of the [[Nature (journal)|Nature]] Video channel on YouTube with over 1.3 million views. This project is part of the ''[[Places & Spaces: Mapping Science]]'' exhibit.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://scimaps.org/mapdetail/charting_culture_186|title=Places & Spaces: Mapping Science|access-date=17 March 2019}}</ref> It was also featured in "The Best American Infographics 2015".<ref name=":0" />
* ''News Explorer'' is a web application providing new interface for news analysis and discovery.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.visualcomplexity.com/vc/project.cfm?id=993|title=Visual Complexity. Watson News Explorer|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref>
* ''Network Earth''<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.fastcompany.com/3056999/how-were-pushing-natures-network-architecture-to-a-catastrophic-crash|title=Fast Company. We're Pushing Nature's Network Architecture To A Catastrophic Crash|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> won the 2017 Best Scientific Video award of the National Science Foundation. ''Network Earth'' explores nature's resilience and interconnections between all life on Earth. It accompanied a research paper published in [[Nature (journal)|Nature]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.nature.com/nature/videoarchive/network_earth/index.html|title=Nature. Universal resilience patterns in complex networks|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref>
* ''Rise of Partisanship'' shows the party [[Polarization (politics)|polarization]] of the [[United States House of Representatives|House of Representatives]] through time.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/wonk/wp/2015/04/23/a-stunning-visualization-of-our-divided-congress/?amp;utm_term=.a8773a78927c&noredirect=on|title=The Washington Post. A stunning visualization of our divided Congress|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.businessinsider.com/animation-rise-partisanship-congress-house-representatives-60-years-2016-4|title=Business Insider. This 60-second animation shows how divided Congress has become over the last 60 years|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref> This project was included in "The Best American Infographics 2016".<ref name=":1" />
* ''Redrawing the map of Great Britain from a network of human interactions'' explored a new approach to regional delineation, based on analyzing networks of billions of individual human transactions.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://senseable.mit.edu/network/network&society2.html|title=Senseable City Lab MIT|access-date=18 March 2019}}</ref>
