'''Wearsafe''' is an American personal safety company located in [[Hartford, Connecticut|Hartford]], CT. Their primary [[wearable technology]] is a device that uses [[bluetooth]] technology to connect a button-like Tag with a smartphone app.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.courant.com/hometown-heroes/hc-hometown-hero-wearsafe-labs-david-benoit-20160124-story.html|title=CT Duo Create Wearable Safety Tag Inspired By 2007 Cheshire Home Invasion|last=Courant|first=Hartford|date=23 January 2016 |access-date=2016-09-06}}</ref> When pressed, the Tag alerts a user's selected network of the location of the emergency, as well as sending a live audio feed of the incident to a group chat function. Wearsafe's mission is to "make the world a safer place by redefining the way people seek and provide help"-- by reaching a wide variety of customers, including outdoors sportsmen and anyone looking to control their personal safety, including preventing [[sexual assault]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.digitaltrends.com/wearables/wearsafe-tag/|title=The Wearsafe Tag could be a game-changer in fighting sexual assault|date=2016-03-06|language=en-US|access-date=2016-09-06}}</ref>

== History and Mission ==
Wearsafe was created in response to the [[Cheshire, Connecticut, home invasion murders|Cheshire Home Invasion Murders]]  of 2007. The founders, David Benoit and Phillip Giancarlo, began work on the technology in 2011 with copatent providers Rick Borden, Kyle Busque and Keven Busque.  The company has received significant investments from Ravi Singh, Yogasmogo and other investors.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.hartfordbusiness.com/article/20151210/NEWS01/151219999/wearsafe-labs-lands-nearly-3m-investment|title=Wearsafe Labs lands nearly $3M investment|language=en-US|access-date=2016-09-06}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.techinsider.io/yogasmoga-invested-in-wearsafe-panic-buttons-2016-3|title=Yoga pants of the future may have panic buttons|access-date=2016-09-06}}</ref> The goal of Wearsafe Labs is to "connect students with trusted contacts, enabling them to feel safe wherever they are."<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.campussafetymagazine.com/article/what_can_help_make_students_and_their_families_instantly_feel_safer_and_mor#|title=Making Students and Their Families Feel Safer and More Confident on Any College Campus|last=Staff|first=CS|website=www.campussafetymagazine.com|date=22 July 2016 |access-date=2016-09-06}}</ref>  Wearsafe is designed to both aid victims in an emergency and deter attackers. According to [[Digital Trends]], former U.S. Secret Service [[special agent]] Rich Starpoli, who served Presidents [[George W. Bush]] and [[Bill Clinton]], says “with the knowledge that students could be wearing a Wearsafe-connected device, a college or university creates a significant deterring effect.”<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.digitaltrends.com/wearables/wearsafe-tag/|title=The Wearsafe Tag could be a game-changer in fighting sexual assault|date=2016-03-06|language=en-US|access-date=2016-09-06}}</ref>

== Products ==

=== Tag ===

The Wearsafe Tag is a small, wearable button that operates similarly to other wearable personal safety devices: by connecting to a [[smartphone]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.cnet.com/au/news/silicon-valley-wearables-company-nimb-hopes-smart-ring-will-keep-you-safe/|title=This startup has a smart ring designed to keep you safe|access-date=2016-09-06}}</ref> The device also works without a smartphone, but is limited to 200 ft (70 m) from the location.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.humavox.com/blog/wearable-month-wearsafe-safety-clip/|title=WearSafe Safety Clip|access-date=2015-09-15}}</ref> According to the [[The Boston Globe|Boston Globe]], "Activating the Wearsafe tag not only triggers a set of alerts sent to a designated list of contacts, but sends live location information and audio from the scene and opens a 'virtual situation room' between respondents in order to coordinate help (or call 911). The 'Rewind' function [plays] audio recorded in the 60 seconds prior to activation to provide context. Whatever action taken on a call for distress, the tag vibrates the reassure the caller that help is on the way:<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.bostonglobe.com/lifestyle/2016/08/18/how-wearable-devices-can-keep-you-safe/oX2CpFUTFQUa9FTz5Km13I/story.html|title=How wearable devices can keep you safe - The Boston Globe|website=[[The Boston Globe]] |access-date=2016-09-06}}</ref> The Wearsafe Tag [[Data buffer|buffer]]s live audio whenever it is turned on.

=== App ===

Wearsafe's Tag pairs with a [[mobile app]] to connect with the user's selected network. The Wearsafe Tag sends GPS coordinates and audio context to the network. In an article in [[Law Technology Today]], Attorney N. Kane Bennett, a former Massachusetts prosecutor and current litigator in civil matters, concluded that “an audio recording from the actual incident, in real time, is incredibly powerful evidence in a criminal or civil case.”<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.lawtechnologytoday.org/2016/08/wearsafe-changing-assault-cases/|title=Wearsafe May Change Assault Cases Everywhere - Law Technology Today|website=www.lawtechnologytoday.org|date=18 August 2016 |language=en-US|access-date=2017-03-14}}</ref> The app provides reassurance to the victim that help is on the way-- which Secret Service Agents, Navy SEALS, and CIA Agents "pointed out that people who have reassurance that help is on the way have a much, much higher probability of a successful outcome in a stressful situation."<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.inc.com/kevin-j-ryan/wearsafe-tag-helps-people-in-distress.html|title=How This Startup Built a Wearable for Worst-Case Scenarios|date=2017-08-22|work=Inc.com|access-date=2017-03-14|language=en}}</ref>
