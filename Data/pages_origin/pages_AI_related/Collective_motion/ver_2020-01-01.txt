'''Collective motion''' is defined as the spontaneous [[emergence]] of ordered movement in a system consisting of many [[Self-propelled particles|self-propelled agents]]. It can be observed in everyday life, for example in [[flocks of birds]], [[Shoaling and schooling|schools of fish]], herds of animals and also in crowds and car traffic. It also appears at the microscopic level: in colonies of bacteria, motility assays and artificial [[self-propelled particles]].<ref>Palacci, Jeremie; Sacanna, Stefano; Steinberg, Asher Preska; Pine, David J.; Chaikin, Paul M. (2013-02-22). "Living Crystals of Light-Activated Colloidal Surfers". Science 339 (6122): 936–940. {{doi|10.1126/science.1230020}}. {{ISSN|0036-8075}}. {{PMID|23371555}}.</ref><ref>Theurkauff, I., Cottin-Bizonne, C., Palacci, J., Ybert, C., & Bocquet, L. (2012). Dynamic clustering in active colloidal suspensions with chemical signaling. Physical Review Letters, 108(26), 268303.</ref><ref>Buttinoni, I., Bialké, J., Kümmel, F., Löwen, H., Bechinger, C., & Speck, T. (2013). Dynamical clustering and phase separation in suspensions of self-propelled colloidal particles. Physical review letters, 110(23), 238301.</ref> The scientific community is trying to understand the [[Universality (dynamical systems)|universality]] of this phenomenon. In particular it is intensively investigated in [[statistical physics]] and in the field of [[active matter]]. Experiments on animals,<ref>Feder, Toni (October 2007). "Statistical physics is for the birds". Physics Today 60 (10): 28–30. {{doi|10.1063/1.2800090}}</ref> biological and synthesized [[self-propelled particles]], simulations<ref>{{Cite journal|title = Onset of Collective and Cohesive Motion|journal = Physical Review Letters|date = 2004-01-15|pages = 025702|volume = 92|issue = 2|doi = 10.1103/PhysRevLett.92.025702|pmid = 14753946|first = Guillaume|last = Grégoire|first2 = Hugues|last2 = Chaté|arxiv = cond-mat/0401208 |bibcode = 2004PhRvL..92b5702G }}</ref> and theories<ref>{{Cite journal|title = Long-Range Order in a Two-Dimensional Dynamical $\mathrm{XY}$ Model: How Birds Fly Together|journal = Physical Review Letters|date = 1995-12-04|pages = 4326–4329|volume = 75|issue = 23|doi = 10.1103/PhysRevLett.75.4326|pmid = 10059876|first = John|last = Toner|first2 = Yuhai|last2 = Tu|bibcode = 1995PhRvL..75.4326T }}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|title = Modeling collective motion: variations on the Vicsek model|journal = The European Physical Journal B|date = 2008-07-11|issn = 1434-6028|pages = 451–456|volume = 64|issue = 3–4|doi = 10.1140/epjb/e2008-00275-9|language = en|first = H.|last = Chaté|first2 = F.|last2 = Ginelli|first3 = G.|last3 = Grégoire|first4 = F.|last4 = Peruani|first5 = F.|last5 = Raynaud|bibcode = 2008EPJB...64..451C }}</ref> are conducted in parallel to study these phenomena. ==Collective behavior of Self-propelled particles<ref>{{Cite book|title=Collective Behaviour of Artificial Microswimmers in Response to Environmental Conditions|last=Altemose|first=A; Sen, A.|publisher=Royal Society of Chemistry|year=2018|isbn=9781788011662|location=|pages=250–283}}</ref>==

Just like biological systems in nature, [[self-propelled particles]] also respond to external gradients and show collective behavior. Micromotors or [[nanomotor]]s can interact with self-generated gradients and exhibit schooling and exclusion behavior.<ref>Wang, W., Duan, W., Ahmed, S., Mallouk, T., & Sen, A. (2013). Small power: Autonomous nano- and micromotors propelled by self-generated gradients. NanoToday, 8(5), 531.</ref> For example, Ibele, ''et al.'' demonstrated that silver chloride micromotors, in the presence of UV light, interact with each other at high concentrations and form schools.<ref>Ibele, M., Mallouk, T., & Sen, A. (2009). Schooling behavior of light-powered autonomous micromotors in water. Angewandte Chemie International Edition, 48(18), 3308.</ref> Similar behavior can also be observed with titanium dioxide microparticles.<ref>Hong, Y., Diaz, M., Córdova‐Figueroa, U., & Sen, A. (2010). Light‐Driven Titanium‐Dioxide‐Based Reversible Microfireworks and Micromotor/Micropump Systems. Advanced Functional Materials, 20(10), 1568.</ref> Silver orthophosphate microparticles exhibit transitions between schooling and exclusion behaviors in response to ammonia, hydrogen peroxide, and UV light.<ref>Duan, W., Liu, R., & Sen, A. (2013). Transition between collective behaviors of micromotors in response to different stimuli. Journal of American Chemical Society, 135, 1280.</ref><ref>Altemose, A., Sánchez-Farrán, M. A., Duan, W., Schulz, S., Borhan, A., Crespi, V. H., & Sen, A. (2017). Chemically-controlled spatiotemporal oscillations of colloidal assemblies. Angewandte Chemie International Edition. {{doi|10.1002/anie.201703239}}.</ref> This behavior can be used to design a NOR gate since different combinations of the two different stimuli (ammonia and UV light) generate different outputs. Oscillations between schooling and exclusion behaviors are also tunable via changes in hydrogen peroxide concentration.

Micromotors and nanomotors can also move preferentially in the direction of externally applied chemical gradients, a phenomenon defined as [[chemotaxis]]. Chemotaxis has been observed in self-propelled Au-Pt nanorods, which diffuse towards the source of hydrogen peroxide, when placed in a gradient of the chemical.<ref>Hong, Y., Blackmann, NMK., Kopp, ND., Sen, A., & Velegol, D. (2007). Chemotaxis of nonbiological colloidal rods. Physical Review Letters, 99, 178103.</ref> Silica microparticles with Grubbs catalyst tethered to them, also move towards higher monomer concentrations.<ref>Ravlick, RA., Sengupta, S., McFadden, T., Zhang, H., & Sen, A. (2011). A Polymerization-Powered Motor. Angewandte Chemie International Edition, 50(40), 9374.</ref> Enzymes also behave as [[nanomotor]]s and migrate towards regions of higher substrate concentration, which is known as enzyme chemotaxis.<ref>Sengupta, S., Dey, KK., Muddana, HS., Tabouillot, T., Ibele, M., Butler, PJ., & Sen, A.
