'''Basic Formal Ontology''' ('''BFO''') is a [[Upper ontology|top-level ontology]] developed by [[Barry Smith (academic)|Barry Smith]] and his associates for the purposes of promoting interoperability among domain ontologies built in its terms through a process of downward population.  A guide to building BFO-conformant domain ontologies was published by MIT Press in 2015.<ref>{{cite book |first1=Robert |last1=Arp |first2=Barry |last2=Smith |first3=Andrew D. |last3=Spear |title=Building Ontologies with Basic Formal Ontology |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=AUxQCgAAQBAJ |date=2015 |publisher=MIT Press |isbn=978-0-262-52781-1}}</ref> 

The ontology arose against the background of research in ontologies in the domain of geospatial information science by David Mark, Pierre Grenon, Achille Varzi and others,<ref>{{cite journal |last1=Grenon |first1=P. |last2=Smith |first2=B. |title=SNAP and SPAN: Towards Dynamic Spatial Ontology |journal=Spatial Cognition and Computation |volume=4 |issue=1 |pages=69–103 |year=2004 |doi=10.1207/s15427633scc0401_5 |s2cid=14469822 |url=http://ontology.buffalo.edu/smith/articles/SNAP_SPAN.pdf}}</ref> with a special role for the study of vagueness and of the ways sharp boundaries in the geospatial and other domains are created by fiat.<ref>{{cite journal |last1=Smith |first1=Barry |last2=Varzi |first2=Achille C. |title=Fiat and bona fide boundaries: Towards an ontology of spatially extended objects |journal=International Conference on Spatial Information Theory (COSIT) |volume= 30 |issue=3 |pages=103-119 |year=2003 |url= https://philarchive.org/archive/SMIFAB-3}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |last1=Smith |first1=Barry |last2=Mark |first2=David M. |title=Do mountains exist? Towards an ontology of landforms |journal=Environment and Planning B |volume= 30 |issue=3 |pages=411-428 |year=2003 |url= https://philarchive.org/archive/SMIDME-2}}</ref> 

BFO has passed through 4 major releases, documented [https://ncorwiki.buffalo.edu/index.php/BFO_Release_History:_From_BFO_1_to_BFO-ISO here]. The current revision was released in 2020,<ref name="BFO">{{cite web |url=http://basic-formal-ontology.org/bfo-2020.html |title=Basic Formal Ontology (BFO) 2020 |access-date=June 24, 2021}}</ref> and this forms the basis of the standard,{{cite web|title=ISO/IEC 21838-2|url=https://standards.iso.org/iso-iec/21838/-2/ed-1/en}} which was released by the Joint Committee of the International Standards Organization and International Electrotechnical Commission in 2021.

The structure of BFO is based on a division of entities into two disjoint categories of ''continuant'' and ''occurrent'', the former consists of objects and spatial regions, the latter contains processes conceived as extended through (or spanning) time. BFO thereby seeks to consolidate both time and space within a single framework.

==Applications==

BFO has been adopted as a foundational ontology by over 450 ontology projects,<ref>{{cite web |title=Ontologies and institutions/groups using BFO.|url=http://basic-formal-ontology.org/users.html |website=Basic Formal Ontology (BFO) |access-date=December 2, 2022}}</ref> principally in the areas of biomedical ontology,<ref>[[Open Biomedical Ontologies Foundry]] (OBO).</ref> security and defense (intelligence) ontology,<ref>Peter Morosoff, Ron Rudnicki, Jason Bryant, Robert Farrell and Barry Smith, "[https://philpapers.org/rec/MORJDO Joint Doctrine Ontology: A Benchmark for Military Information Systems Interoperability]", in ''Semantic Technology for Intelligence, Defense and Security'' (STIDS). CEUR vol. 1325. pp. 2-9 (2015).</ref> and industry ontologies<ref>[https://www.industrialontologies.org/ Industrial Ontologies Foundry]</ref>.<ref name="BFO"/> Example applications of BFO can be seen in the [[Ontology for Biomedical Investigations]] (OBI). 

In 2021, the standard ISO/IEC 21838-2:2021 Information technology — Top-level ontologies (TLO) — Part 2: Basic Formal Ontology (BFO) was published by the Joint Technical Committee of the International Standards Organization and the International Electrotechnical Commission.<ref>https://www.iso.org/standard/74572.html.</ref> ISO/IEC 21838 is a multi-part standard. Part 1 of the standard<ref>https://www.iso.org/standard/71954.html.</ref> specifies the requirements that must be met if an ontology is to be classified as a top-level ontology in accordance with the standard.
