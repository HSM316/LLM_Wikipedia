'''EmoSpark''' is an [[artificial intelligence]] console created in [[London, United Kingdom]] by Patrick Levy-Rosenthal. The device uses facial recognition and language analysis to [[emotion recognition|evaluate human emotion]] and convey responsive content according to the emotion.<ref name=wired>{{cite magazine|title=Emospark is an emotionally-aware AI console for the home|url=https://www.wired.co.uk/news/archive/2014-01/03/emospark|magazine=Wired|accessdate=16 November 2014}}</ref> The console measures 90&nbsp;mm x 90&nbsp;mm x 90&nbsp;mm and is cube shaped. It operates on an "Emotional Processing Unit", an emotion chip developed by Emoshape Inc. that enables the system to create emotional profile graphs of its surroundings.<ref name=wired/> The emotional processing unit is a patent pending technology that is said to create synthesised emotional responses in machines.<ref name=herald>{{cite web |title=Emoshape Provide Intelligent Machines with Emotions |url=http://www.heraldonline.com/2014/11/03/6486612_emoshape-provide-intelligent-machines.html?rh=1 |work=Herald |accessdate=16 November 2014 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20141108144649/http://www.heraldonline.com/2014/11/03/6486612_emoshape-provide-intelligent-machines.html?rh=1 |archivedate=8 November 2014 }}</ref> EmoSpark was funded through an [[Indiegogo]] campaign which aimed to raise $200,000.<ref>{{cite web|title=Emospark First AI Home Console|url=https://www.indiegogo.com/projects/emospark-first-a-i-home-console|publisher=Indiegogo|accessdate=16 November 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Series 20: Episode 11 - EmoSpark|url=http://gadgetshow.channel5.com/news/series-20-episode-11-emospark|publisher=Gadget Show|accessdate=14 December 2014|archive-date=10 December 2014|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141210191358/http://gadgetshow.channel5.com/news/series-20-episode-11-emospark|url-status=dead}}</ref>

==Product overview==
[[File:EmoSPARKcube.jpg|thumb|250px|EmoSPARK Cube]]
EmoSpark was created by French inventor Patrick Levy-Rosenthal, as an emotionally intelligent artificial life unit for the home that can interact with people. It is powered by [[Android (operating system)|Android]] and can communicate with users through typed input from a computer, tablet, smartphone or TV as well as through spoken commands.<ref name=wired/>

The EmoSpark's features are categorized into two types: functional and emotional.<ref name=":0">{{Cite news|url=https://www.cnet.com/news/emospark-ai-console-wants-to-be-your-robot-companion/|title=EmoSpark AI console wants to be your robot companion|work=CNET|access-date=2017-10-06|language=en}}</ref> EmoSpark is said to have the ability to perform practical software-based tasks.<ref name=":0" /> Through the smartphone interface, it is able to gauge a personâ€™s emotions and is reported to have a conversational library of over 2 million sentences.<ref>{{cite web|title=Emospark |url=http://www.bing.com/videos/watch/video/emospark/15rddtf |publisher=Connected Life |accessdate=16 November 2014 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20141214175729/http://www.bing.com/videos/watch/video/emospark/15rddtf |archivedate=14 December 2014 }}</ref> The face-tracking technology identifies users likes and dislikes to categorize their emotional responses to stimuli such as videos and music.<ref name=wired/> The device has an emotional spectrum that is composed of eight emotions which are ''surprise, sadness, joy, trust, fear, disgust, anger and anticipation''.<ref name=wired/>

EmoSpark monitors a person's facial expressions and emotions through images from an external camera, which are then processed through an emotion text analysis and content analysis.<ref name=CNET>{{cite web|title=EmoSpark AI console just wants you to be happy |url=http://www.cnet.com/news/emospark-ai-console-just-wants-you-to-be-happy/|publisher=CNET News |accessdate=16 November 2014 }}</ref> The [[New Scientist]] reported that EmoSpark had the ability to work on the best way to cheer up its users, emotionally.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.newscientist.com/article/mg22630212-900-say-hello-to-machines-that-read-your-emotions-to-make-you-happy/|title=Say hello to machines that read your emotions to make you happy|work=New Scientist|access-date=2017-10-06|language=en-US}}</ref>

===Connectivity===
[[File:EmoSPARKcubeandbox.jpg|thumb|350px|EmoSPARK Cube and Packaging]]
EmoSpark is able to connect to [[Facebook]] and [[YouTube]] to present users with content designed to improve their mood, or to [[Wikipedia]] for collaborative knowledge that can be shared when users ask questions of it. Through Android OS, EmoSpark is able to be customized with [[Google Play]] store apps.<ref name=wired/>

The cube is expected to develop its own personality based on the communications it has had with the people using it.<ref name=roboticstomorrow>{{cite web|title=The EmoSPARK Cube Is Bringing the Fascinating World of AI to Our Personal Lives|url=http://www.roboticstomorrow.com/news/2014/10/15/indiegogo--the-emospark-cube-is-bringing-the-fascinating-world-of-ai-to-our-personal-lives-/4912/|publisher=Robotics Tomorrow|accessdate=16 November 2014}}</ref>

==EmoShape==
The Emotion Chip (EPU) used in the cube is created by the US company Emoshape Inc, founded by Levy-Rosenthal. EmoShape Ltd (UK) was the company that developed EmoSpark cube. Patrick Levy-Rosenthal also received the IST Prize in 2005 from the European Council for Applied Science, Technology and Engineering.<ref>{{cite web|title=Emoshape Ltd is pushing the boundaries of interactivity. The company is dedicated to providing powerful and easy-to-use multimedia emotional technology.|url=https://www.linkedin.com/company/emoshape-ltd|publisher=LinkedIn|accessdate=16 November 2014}}</ref>
